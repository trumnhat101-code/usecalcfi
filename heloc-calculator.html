How a HELOC Works

A HELOC has two phases. During the draw period (typically 10 years), you can borrow up to your credit limit repeatedly — like a credit card. You only pay interest on what you've drawn. During the repayment period (typically 20 years), the line closes and you repay principal + interest on the outstanding balance.

HELOC vs. Home Equity Loan

  • HELOC: Variable rate, revolving line, interest-only during draw period, flexible borrowing. Best for ongoing expenses like home renovations over time.
  • Home Equity Loan: Fixed rate, lump sum, fixed monthly payment from day one. Best for a single large expense with a known amount.

Using a HELOC for Home Improvements

Interest on a HELOC is tax-deductible when the funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home securing the loan (IRS Publication 936). Keep receipts and consult a tax professional. Interest for other purposes (debt consolidation, vacations) is generally not deductible.

💡 HELOC Tips

Shop multiple lenders — rates vary by 1–2% between banks and credit unions.

Watch out for the repayment shock: draw payments jump significantly when repayment begins.

Some lenders charge annual fees ($50–100) and closing costs (0–2%).
