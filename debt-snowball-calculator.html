    How the debt snowball works

    The debt snowball method has four steps:

    • Step 1: List all debts from smallest to largest balance (ignoring interest rates)
    • Step 2: Pay the minimum on every debt
    • Step 3: Put every extra dollar toward the smallest debt
    • Step 4: When the smallest debt is paid off, roll its full payment + minimums into the next debt

    The key insight is momentum. Each debt paid off frees up more money for the next one — and the psychological win of eliminating a debt keeps you motivated.

    Snowball vs. Avalanche — which is better?

    Mathematically, the avalanche method (highest rate first) always saves more interest and pays off debt slightly faster. But research shows that many people succeed with the snowball precisely because of the motivation from quick wins.

    The best method is the one you'll actually stick with. If you have high-rate debts with similar balances, the avalanche saves meaningfully more. If you have one small balance you can knock out in 2–3 months, the snowball jumpstart can be worth it.

    How to find extra money for debt payoff

    • Cut one subscription: Canceling a $15–25/month service and redirecting it to debt adds up fast
    • Use windfalls: Tax refunds, bonuses, and gifts make excellent lump-sum debt payments
    • The 50/30/20 rule: Allocate 20% of take-home pay to debt payoff and savings
    • Sell unused items: A weekend of selling unused items on Facebook Marketplace can generate $200–500
    • Negotiate lower rates: Call credit card companies to request a rate reduction — it works more often than you'd think

    Frequently asked questions

    Should I use savings to pay off debt?

    Keep a $1,000 emergency fund first (Dave Ramsey's Baby Step 1). After that, if your debt rate exceeds what your savings earns (usually true for anything above 4–5% APR), putting savings toward debt is mathematically better. Don't deplete your emergency fund entirely — unexpected expenses will put you right back into debt.

    Does the debt snowball work for mortgages?

    The snowball typically covers consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans, auto loans, student loans). Most people keep their mortgage out of the plan and tackle it as a separate "Baby Step 6" after all other debt is cleared.

    How does debt consolidation fit in?

    If you can consolidate high-rate credit card debt into a lower-rate personal loan, you reduce the total interest while still applying the snowball/avalanche strategy to the consolidated loan. This works best when the personal loan rate is significantly lower (20%+ card → 10–12% loan).
