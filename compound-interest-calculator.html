How compound interest works

With simple interest, you earn interest only on your original principal. With compound interest, you earn interest on your principal plus all previously earned interest. This creates exponential growth — the longer the time horizon, the more dramatic the effect.

The compound interest formula is: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) + PMT × ((1 + r/n)^(nt) − 1) / (r/n)

  • A = final amount
  • P = principal (initial deposit)
  • r = annual interest rate (decimal)
  • n = compounding periods per year
  • t = time in years
  • PMT = regular contribution per period

The power of starting early

Time is the most powerful variable in compound interest. Consider two investors at 7% annual return:

  • Investor A starts at 25, invests $200/month until 65 = $525,000
  • Investor B starts at 35, invests $200/month until 65 = $243,000

Starting 10 years earlier more than doubles the result, even though Investor A only contributed $24,000 more.

Compounding frequency: does it matter?

More frequent compounding earns slightly more interest. On $10,000 at 7% for 10 years: annual compounding yields $19,672; monthly compounding yields $20,097; daily compounding yields $20,136. The difference is marginal at typical savings rates but significant for higher rates or longer periods.

Frequently asked questions

What is the Rule of 72?

Divide 72 by your annual return rate to estimate the number of years to double your money. At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 9%: 72 ÷ 9 = 8 years. It's a quick mental math shortcut accurate within 1–2% for rates between 4–15%.

Where can I earn compound interest?

High-yield savings accounts (HYSA), CDs, money market accounts, I-bonds, and investment accounts (index funds, ETFs) all benefit from compounding. Investment returns compound through price appreciation and reinvested dividends. HYSAs currently offer 4–5% APY (rates change — always compare current offers).

What's the difference between APR and APY?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the simple annual rate without compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) includes the effect of compounding and is always slightly higher than APR. When comparing savings accounts, use APY for apples-to-apples comparison.
