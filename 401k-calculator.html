How to maximize your 401k

The 401k is one of the most powerful retirement savings tools available — contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-deferred, and employer matches are essentially free money.

Always capture the full employer match

If your employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary, contribute at least 6%. Anything less leaves free money behind. A $4,500 match on a $75,000 salary is a 6% instant return before any market gains.

2025 contribution limits

The IRS 401k contribution limit for 2025 is $23,500 ($31,000 if you're 50+ with catch-up contributions). Employer contributions don't count toward this limit.

Traditional vs Roth 401k

Traditional 401k: contributions are pre-tax, reducing your taxable income now. You pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement. Roth 401k: contributions are after-tax, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, Roth may be better.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I contribute to my 401k?

At minimum, contribute enough to get the full employer match. Beyond that, aim for 10–15% of gross income (including the match). Max out if possible — $23,500 in 2025.

What is a good annual return for a 401k?

6–7% is a reasonable long-term assumption for a diversified stock-heavy portfolio, adjusted for inflation. The S&P 500 has averaged ~10% historically before inflation.

Traditional vs. Roth 401k: Which Is Better?

The choice depends on whether you expect your tax rate to be higher now or in retirement. Traditional 401k lowers your taxable income today and defers taxes until withdrawal. Roth 401k uses after-tax dollars but withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free — including all the growth.

Early in your career, when income and tax rates are typically lower, Roth often wins. As income rises, Traditional becomes more attractive. Many advisors recommend splitting contributions between both to hedge against future tax uncertainty.

The Power of Starting Early

At 7% annual growth, $10,000 invested at age 25 becomes ~$150,000 by age 65. The same $10,000 invested at age 45 grows to only ~$39,000. Starting 20 years earlier gives you nearly 4x the outcome on the same initial amount — that's compound growth working for you.

Even small increases matter. Boosting contributions by just 1% of a $60,000 salary ($600/yr) at age 30 adds roughly $75,000 to your 401k by retirement at 65.

What Happens to Your 401k If You Change Jobs?

You have four options: leave it with your old employer, roll it into your new employer's plan, roll it into an IRA, or cash it out. Cashing out triggers income taxes plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you're under 59½ — typically the worst option. Rolling into an IRA gives you the most investment flexibility and control. Be sure to check vesting schedules — unvested employer match may not come with you.
