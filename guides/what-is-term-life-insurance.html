What Is Term Life Insurance? 2026 Guide to Rates & Coverage

By Nathan Le

Quick answer: Term life insurance covers you for a set period — typically 10, 20, or 30 years — and pays a tax-free death benefit if you die during the term. It's the cheapest type of life insurance because it builds no cash value: a healthy 35-year-old can get $500,000 of 20-year coverage for roughly $25–$35/month.

Term life is the simplest, most affordable way to protect your family. Here's how much you need, what it costs, and how to buy it right.

What Is Term Life Insurance?

Term life insurance pays a tax-free death benefit to your beneficiaries if you die during the policy term. You pay a fixed monthly or annual premium for a set period — typically 10, 15, 20, or 30 years. If you outlive the term, the policy expires with no payout (like car insurance — you don't get a refund for not crashing).

Who needs it: Anyone with dependents (spouse, children, aging parents) or financial obligations (mortgage, business debts) who would struggle financially if you died. If nobody depends on your income, you may not need life insurance at all.

Term vs Whole Life: Which Should You Buy?

✅ Term Life (Recommended for Most)

  • Fixed term: 10, 15, 20, or 30 years
  • Much lower premiums
  • Pure death benefit — no investment component
  • Best for income replacement while dependents are young
  • $500K coverage, age 35: ~$25–35/month

⚠️ Whole Life (Rarely Worth It)

  • Permanent coverage — never expires
  • Builds cash value (low returns, 2–4%)
  • 5–15x more expensive per dollar of coverage
  • $500K coverage, age 35: ~$400–600/month
  • Useful in narrow estate planning scenarios only

For most families, the advice is: buy term, invest the difference. The premium savings between term and whole life invested in index funds will almost always outperform the whole life cash value.

How to Buy Term Life Insurance

  1. Calculate how much coverage you needUse the formula above. Round up — coverage is cheap and you can't buy more after a health change.
  2. Choose your term lengthMatch the term to when your financial obligations end. Kids' college + mortgage payoff is the most common benchmark.
  3. Get quotes from multiple insurersRates vary 30–50% between companies for the same person. Use a broker or comparison site to check 5+ insurers.
  4. Complete the application and medical examMost policies require a paramedical exam (blood pressure, blood draw, urine sample — done at your home for free). No-exam policies exist but cost more.
  5. Name your beneficiaries carefullyName specific people, not "my estate." Keep beneficiary designations updated after major life events (divorce, new children).

When Does It Make Sense to Skip Life Insurance?

  • You are single with no dependents and no co-signed debts
  • Your savings and assets could cover all dependents' needs
  • Your children are grown and financially independent
  • Your mortgage is paid off and spouse earns enough independently

Common Term Life Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying too little coverage — $250K feels like a lot but won't last long replacing a salary + mortgage
  • Choosing too short a term — A 10-year term at 40 leaves you uninsured at 50 with young kids and a big mortgage
  • Buying employer-only coverage — Group life ends when you leave. Own your own policy independently.
  • Waiting too long — Every year of delay raises premiums. A health event can make you uninsurable.
  • Naming your estate as beneficiary — Goes through probate, subject to creditors. Name a person instead.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does term life insurance cost?
A healthy 35-year-old can get a $500,000 20-year term policy for roughly $25–35/month. Rates depend on age, health, coverage amount, and term length.
How much life insurance do I need?
A common rule is 10–12x your annual income. A more precise method: add your mortgage balance, income replacement for 10+ years, future college costs, and final expenses, then subtract existing savings.
What is the difference between term and whole life insurance?
Term life covers you for a set period (10–30 years) and is much cheaper. Whole life covers you permanently and builds cash value, but costs 5–15x more per dollar of coverage. Most financial experts recommend term.
What happens when my term life insurance expires?
The policy ends. You can renew at much higher rates, convert to permanent coverage (if your policy has this rider), or let it lapse. Many people no longer need coverage once the term ends — mortgage is paid, kids are grown.
Can I get life insurance with pre-existing conditions?
Yes, but you'll likely pay higher premiums. Well-controlled conditions like diabetes or high blood pressure may still qualify for standard rates. Shop multiple insurers — underwriting criteria vary significantly.
