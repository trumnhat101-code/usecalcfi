The IRS sets annual limits on how much you can contribute to tax-advantaged retirement accounts. In 2026, SECURE 2.0 introduced a special "super catch-up" for ages 60–63.

🧭 Why Most Americans Are Behind — and What Actually Works

The Savings Crisis Is Real, But Not Hopeless

The data shows a stark gap: the median 55–64 year old has $193,500 — roughly 37% of what Fidelity recommends. But "behind" doesn't mean "doomed." Social Security replaces about 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners (more for lower earners). Part-time work in early retirement, downsizing housing, or retiring to a lower cost-of-living state can all significantly reduce the savings needed.

3 High-Impact Moves If You're Behind

1. Maximize catch-up contributions. If you're 50+, you can contribute up to $31,000/year to a 401(k) in 2026 — and $34,750 at ages 60–63. Maxing out for just 10 years at 7% growth adds over $400,000.

2. Delay Social Security. For every year past 62 you wait to claim Social Security (up to age 70), your benefit increases 6–8%. Claiming at 70 vs 62 results in roughly double the monthly benefit. For someone with modest savings, this can be more impactful than any investment return.

3. Work 2–3 extra years. The math is compelling: more contribution time, one less year of withdrawals, and a higher Social Security benefit all compound. A 63-year-old who works until 66 instead of retiring early often sees outcomes dramatically better than someone who retired at 62 with a larger portfolio but smaller SS benefit.

If You're Under 40: Time Is Your Edge

At 30 with $10,000 saved, contributing $500/month at 7% returns reaches $1.1M by 65. At 40 with $10,000 saved, the same $500/month reaches only $487,000. The math isn't about income — it's about how early you start. Even $200/month starting at 25 becomes $525,000 by 65.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average retirement savings by age in 2026?
Based on Federal Reserve data adjusted for 2026: Under 35 ($51,400 avg / $13,500 median), 35–44 ($148,100 / $47,000), 45–54 ($327,100 / $91,300), 55–64 ($561,800 / $193,500), 65–74 ($636,200 / $209,500), 75+ ($481,900 / $136,200). The median is far more representative of the typical American — the average is skewed by the very wealthy.
How much should I have saved for retirement by age 50?
Fidelity recommends 6× your annual salary by age 50. For the U.S. median earner (~$65,000 salary), that's roughly $390,000. The actual median savings for 45–54 year olds is only $91,300 — a gap of nearly $300,000. If you're behind, the catch-up contribution at age 50 ($31,000/year limit in 2026) is your most powerful tool.
Why is average retirement savings so much higher than median?
The average is skewed upward by Americans with $2M–$10M+ in retirement accounts. The median — where half have more, half have less — is more accurate for the typical person. At 65–74, the average is $636,200 but the median is $209,500. Most Americans have significantly less than "average" headlines imply.
What are the 401(k) contribution limits for 2026?
In 2026: Under 50 = $23,500. Ages 50–59 = $31,000 (includes $7,500 catch-up). Ages 60–63 = $34,750 (SECURE 2.0 super catch-up). Ages 64+ = $31,000. The total limit including employer contributions is $70,000. IRAs have a separate $7,000 limit ($8,000 if 50+).
How much do I need to save each month to retire with $1 million?
At 7% average return: Age 25 = $382/mo, Age 30 = $545/mo, Age 35 = $794/mo, Age 40 = $1,195/mo, Age 45 = $1,879/mo, Age 50 = $3,154/mo, Age 55 = $6,138/mo. Every decade you wait roughly doubles the monthly requirement. Use our retirement calculator to model your specific situation.
What is a good retirement savings amount at 60?
Fidelity recommends 8× salary by 60 — approximately $520,000 for median earners. The actual median at 55–64 is $193,500. At 60–63, you can contribute up to $34,750/year to a 401(k) under SECURE 2.0's super catch-up. Also consider: delaying Social Security to 70 can roughly double your monthly benefit compared to claiming at 62, which may be more valuable than additional savings for many people.

📐 Methodology & Data Sources

  • Average and median savings: Based on the Federal Reserve's 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF), the most comprehensive wealth survey in the U.S., conducted every 3 years. 2026 figures are estimated by applying a ~4% upward adjustment to reflect growth in retirement account balances since 2022, based on market returns and contribution trends from Vanguard's How America Saves 2024 report.
  • Fidelity benchmarks: Based on Fidelity's Retirement Savings Guidelines (2024), which recommend 1× salary by 30, 3× by 40, 6× by 50, 8× by 60, and 10× by 67. Benchmarks shown assume the U.S. median household salary of approximately $65,000.
  • Monthly savings to $1M: Calculated using the future value of annuity formula, assuming 7% annual return compounded monthly (approximately the historical average real return of a diversified stock portfolio), targeting $1,000,000 at age 65.
  • 401(k) limits: Per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-XX and SECURE 2.0 Act provisions for the 2026 tax year.
