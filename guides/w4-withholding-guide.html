W-4 Withholding Guide 2026:
How to Fill Out Your W-4 Correctly

By Nathan Le

All 5 steps of the W-4 explained clearly — including multiple jobs, claiming dependents, extra withholding, and when you need to update it.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: IRS Publication 15-T, IRS.gov

The W-4 form controls how much federal income tax your employer withholds from each paycheck. Fill it out correctly and you'll owe little or nothing at tax time. Fill it out wrong and you could face an unexpected tax bill — or an interest-free loan to the IRS. Here's how to do it right.

📋 Note: The W-4 was redesigned in 2020. The old "allowances" system (claiming 0 or 1) no longer exists. The current form uses dollar amounts. This guide covers the current form.

What Is a W-4?

A W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate) is a form you complete for your employer. It tells them how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Your employer sends those withholdings to the IRS on your behalf throughout the year. When you file your tax return, withholdings are credited against your tax bill — resulting in a refund if too much was withheld, or a balance due if too little was.

The 5 Steps of the W-4

STEP 1

Personal Information (Required)

Enter your name, address, Social Security number, and filing status. Filing status options:

  • Single or Married filing separately — higher withholding
  • Married filing jointly — lower withholding (assumes two incomes, standard deduction for married couples)
  • Head of household — lower withholding for qualifying single parents
STEP 2

Multiple Jobs or Spouse Works (Complete if applicable)

Required if you have multiple jobs simultaneously OR are married filing jointly and your spouse also works. Options:

  • Option A (easiest): Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator at IRS.gov — gives you the most accurate result
  • Option B: Complete the Multiple Jobs Worksheet (page 3 of the W-4)
  • Option C (simplest but highest withholding): Check the checkbox in Step 2(c) — only works if you have exactly 2 jobs with similar pay

⚠️ If you skip this step with multiple jobs, you'll be under-withheld and owe taxes at filing.

STEP 3

Claim Dependents (Optional)

Reduces withholding by accounting for the Child Tax Credit and other dependent credits. Only complete on the W-4 for your highest-paying job.

  • Children under 17 who qualify: multiply by $2,000 per child
  • Other dependents (elderly parents, older children): multiply by $500 per dependent
  • Add these amounts together and enter the total
STEP 4

Other Adjustments (Optional)

Fine-tune your withholding for three situations:

  • 4(a) Other income not from jobs: Enter annual investment income, freelance income, retirement distributions you want taxed. This avoids having to make quarterly estimated tax payments.
  • 4(b) Deductions: If you itemize (mortgage interest, large charitable donations, high medical expenses), enter the amount above the standard deduction ($15,000 single / $30,000 married in 2026) to reduce withholding.
  • 4(c) Extra withholding per pay period: If you want more tax withheld each paycheck (to guarantee a refund or cover side income), enter a dollar amount here.
STEP 5

Sign and Date (Required)

Sign the form and give it to your employer's HR or payroll department. Do not send it to the IRS.

W-4 by Situation

Single, one job, no dependents

Fill out Step 1 and Step 5 only. Your withholding will be correct for your tax bracket.

Married, both spouses work

Must complete Step 2. Use the IRS Estimator or Multiple Jobs Worksheet. Don't skip or you'll under-withhold.

Single with a side job / freelance

Use Step 4(a) to add annual freelance income, OR make quarterly estimated tax payments. Both prevent a surprise bill.

Parent with children

Complete Step 3 to claim Child Tax Credit. Reduces withholding and increases take-home pay each paycheck.

Homeowner with mortgage

If your mortgage interest + state taxes + charity exceeds your standard deduction, complete Step 4(b) to reduce withholding.

Want a guaranteed refund

Enter extra withholding in Step 4(c). This builds up a refund but reduces each paycheck — effectively a forced savings account.

When to Update Your W-4

Submit a new W-4 to HR whenever your tax situation changes. Common triggers:

  • Got married or divorced
  • Had a child or adopted
  • Started or stopped a second job
  • Spouse started or stopped working
  • Bought a home (new mortgage interest deduction)
  • Received a large raise or pay cut
  • Owed a large amount or got a very large refund at filing
  • Retired or started receiving pension/Social Security income

There's no limit on how often you can update. Changes take effect within 1-2 pay periods after submission.

Refund vs. Break-Even: What's Better?

A large tax refund feels good but means you over-withheld — you gave the IRS an interest-free loan all year. A $3,000 refund invested at 5% over 12 months = $150 in lost interest. Consider reducing withholding (via Step 3 or adjusting Step 4) and investing the difference each month instead.

That said, many people prefer over-withholding as a forced savings mechanism. If you have trouble saving, a guaranteed annual refund is better than owing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a W-4 form? +
A W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate) tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Withholdings are credited against your tax bill at filing — resulting in a refund (over-withheld) or amount owed (under-withheld).
How do I fill out a W-4 with multiple jobs? +
Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator (easiest, most accurate) or complete the Multiple Jobs Worksheet on page 3 of the W-4. Never just check "Single" on each job — this leads to significant under-withholding and a tax bill at filing.
Should I claim 0 or 1 on my W-4? +
The 2020 redesign eliminated allowances — 0 and 1 no longer apply. The current W-4 uses dollar amounts. For one job with no dependents: just complete Steps 1 and 5. The IRS will withhold the correct amount based on your filing status.
When should I update my W-4? +
Whenever your tax situation changes: marriage, divorce, new child, second job change, home purchase, large raise, or if you owed a lot or got a large refund at filing. Submit a new form to HR anytime — changes apply within 1-2 pay periods.
What happens if I don't fill out a W-4? +
Your employer withholds as if you're single with no adjustments — the highest rate for your income. You'll likely get a refund but have lower take-home pay throughout the year.
