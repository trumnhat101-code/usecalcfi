What Is a 401(k)?
How It Works, 2026 Limits & Rules

By Nathan Le

The complete guide to 401(k) plans — contribution limits, employer match, traditional vs Roth, investment options, and withdrawal rules.

Quick answer: A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement account that lets you invest pre-tax (or Roth) dollars straight from your paycheck, often with an employer match. For 2026 you can contribute up to $23,500 ($31,000 if you're 50 or older). The money grows tax-deferred until you withdraw it in retirement.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 📚 Sources: IRS, Department of Labor

A 401(k) is the most widely used retirement savings account in the US — and one of the most powerful wealth-building tools available to employees. If your employer offers one, using it is almost always the right move. This guide explains everything: how it works, how much you can contribute, and how to make the most of it.

How a 401(k) Works

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement account. Here's the basic flow:

  1. You elect a contribution amount (a percentage of your paycheck or a flat dollar amount)
  2. That money is automatically deducted from your paycheck before you can spend it
  3. Your employer may match a portion of your contributions (free money)
  4. The money is invested in a menu of funds your employer selects (usually index funds, target-date funds)
  5. Investments grow tax-deferred — no taxes on dividends, interest, or capital gains each year
  6. At retirement (age 59½+), you withdraw and pay ordinary income taxes on the distributions

The name "401(k)" comes from the IRS tax code section that authorizes it — Section 401(k).

Employer Matching — Never Leave This on the Table

💰 Employer Match = Instant 50–100% Return

A common match formula: 100% of first 3%, then 50% of next 2%

On a $80,000 salary: contribute 5% ($4,000) → employer adds $3,200 → your account gets $7,200. That's an 80% instant return on your contribution.

Always contribute at least enough to capture the full employer match before doing anything else with your money — paying down low-interest debt, investing in a taxable account, or contributing to an IRA. No investment can beat a guaranteed 50-100% return.

Check your plan documents or ask HR for your exact match formula. Common formulas include:

  • 50% match on up to 6% of salary (3% total match)
  • 100% match on up to 3% of salary (3% total match)
  • Dollar-for-dollar match up to a fixed amount (e.g., $2,000)

Traditional 401(k) vs Roth 401(k)

Many employers now offer both. The key difference is when you pay taxes.

🏦 Traditional 401(k)

  • Contributions are pre-tax
  • Reduces your taxable income today
  • Investments grow tax-deferred
  • Withdrawals in retirement taxed as ordinary income
  • RMDs required starting at age 73
  • Best when: You're in a high tax bracket now and expect lower income in retirement

✨ Roth 401(k)

  • Contributions are after-tax
  • No upfront tax deduction
  • Investments grow tax-free
  • Qualified withdrawals are 100% tax-free
  • RMDs apply (roll to Roth IRA to avoid)
  • Best when: You're early in career, expect higher income/taxes later

When in doubt, younger workers (20s–30s) often benefit more from Roth. Higher earners in peak earning years often benefit more from Traditional. You can split contributions between both if your plan allows.

What Happens to Your 401(k) When You Leave a Job?

You have four options:

  1. Leave it with your old employer. Fine if the plan has good, low-cost funds. Less convenient to manage.
  2. Roll it into your new employer's 401(k). Keeps everything in one place.
  3. Roll it into an IRA (recommended for most). More investment options, potentially lower fees, full control. Do a direct rollover to avoid taxes.
  4. Cash it out (avoid this). You'll owe ordinary income taxes + 10% early withdrawal penalty if under 59½, immediately losing 30-40% of the balance.

Early Withdrawal Penalties & Exceptions

⚠️ Withdrawing before age 59½ triggers a 10% penalty + ordinary income taxes. On a $50,000 withdrawal in the 22% tax bracket: $5,000 penalty + $11,000 in taxes = you keep only $34,000.

Exceptions to the 10% penalty include:

  • Separation from service at age 55+ (Rule of 55)
  • Substantially equal periodic payments (SEPP / 72(t))
  • Total and permanent disability
  • Death (beneficiary withdrawals)
  • Qualified domestic relations order (divorce)
  • Hardship withdrawals (still owe income tax)

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin at age 73. The IRS requires you to withdraw a minimum amount each year based on your account balance and life expectancy tables.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a 401(k)? +
A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement savings account. You contribute pre-tax (traditional) or after-tax (Roth) dollars from your paycheck. Many employers match contributions. Investments grow tax-deferred, and you pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement (Traditional) or withdraw tax-free (Roth).
How much can I contribute to a 401(k) in 2026? +
The 2026 employee contribution limit is $23,500. Age 50-59 and 64+: add $7,500 catch-up = $31,000. Ages 60-63: add $11,250 super catch-up = $34,750. The total limit including employer contributions is $70,000 ($77,500 with catch-up).
What is employer 401(k) matching? +
Free money your employer adds based on your contribution. Common example: 100% match on first 3% of salary, 50% on next 2% = 4% total if you contribute 5%. Always contribute enough to get the full match — it's an instant 50-100% return on your money.
What is the difference between traditional and Roth 401(k)? +
Traditional: pre-tax contributions reduce taxable income now; withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Roth: after-tax contributions (no deduction now); qualified withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free. Choose Roth if you expect higher taxes in retirement; Traditional if you expect lower.
When can I withdraw from my 401(k)? +
Penalty-free at age 59½. Before that: 10% penalty + ordinary income taxes. RMDs begin at 73. Some exceptions apply (Rule of 55, disability, SEPP). Avoid early withdrawals — the combined penalty and tax can erase 30-40% of your savings.
What happens to my 401(k) when I leave a job? +
Options: leave it at old employer, roll to new employer's plan, roll to an IRA (most flexible, recommended for most people), or cash out (not recommended — you'll owe income tax + 10% penalty if under 59½). Do a direct rollover to avoid withholding.
