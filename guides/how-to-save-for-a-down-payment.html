How to Save for a Down Payment (2026):
Timeline, Strategies & Best Accounts

By Nathan Le

How much you actually need, realistic savings timelines by income, the best accounts to use, and down payment assistance programs that can cut your timeline in half.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: CFPB, HUD, Fannie Mae

For most Americans, the down payment is the single biggest barrier to homeownership. But with the right savings strategy — the right account, the right target, and a realistic timeline — it's achievable even on a moderate income. Here's how to do it.

Where to Keep Your Down Payment Savings

Critical rule: Never put your down payment in the stock market if you need it within 3 years. A 30% market drop right before you buy could delay your purchase by years.

🏆 High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)

4–5% APY

Best option for most buyers. FDIC insured, no lock-up, earns 10x+ more than traditional savings. Examples: Marcus, Ally, SoFi, Discover.

🏆 Money Market Account

4–5% APY

Similar to HYSA. FDIC insured, often includes check-writing. Good option at the same banks/credit unions.

Treasury Bills / I-Bonds

4–5% APY

US government backed. T-Bills mature in 4–52 weeks. I-Bonds have a 1-year lock-up and $10K annual limit but protect against inflation.

CD (Certificate of Deposit)

4–5% APY

Locks money for 3–24 months. Good if you have a firm timeline. Early withdrawal penalty applies. Use a CD ladder if unsure of exact timing.

Down Payment Assistance Programs

DPA programs can cut your savings timeline dramatically. Most first-time buyers don't know these exist.

  • State Housing Finance Agency (HFA) grants: Many states offer 2-5% grants that don't require repayment. Typically require completing a homebuyer education course.
  • Forgivable loans: Provided as a second mortgage, forgiven after 3-10 years if you remain in the home.
  • Employer homebuyer programs: Some large employers, hospitals, and universities offer DPA for employees buying near work.
  • Fannie Mae HomeReady / Freddie Mac Home Possible: Allow 3% down with reduced PMI premiums and income limits at 80% of area median.
  • Local programs: Counties and cities often have their own programs for teachers, first responders, and low-to-moderate income buyers.

Search your state + "first time homebuyer assistance" or visit HUD.gov to find programs in your area. Income limits typically cap at 80-120% of Area Median Income (AMI).

Step-by-Step Savings Plan

1

Set your target number

Research home prices in your target area. Calculate 5% down + 3% closing costs = 8% of purchase price as your savings goal. Add 1-2% extra as a buffer.

2

Open a dedicated HYSA

Keep down payment savings separate from your emergency fund. A named "Home Fund" account prevents you from dipping into it. Automate monthly transfers.

3

Automate contributions on payday

Set the transfer for the day after your paycheck hits. Out of sight, out of mind. Treat it like a bill you can't skip.

4

Find windfalls and put them to work

Tax refunds, bonuses, gift money, and side income can dramatically accelerate your timeline. Direct 100% of windfalls to your home fund.

5

Research DPA programs 12 months out

Many programs require completing a HUD-approved homebuyer education course ($25-75). Do this a year in advance so you're ready to apply when you find a home.

6

Get pre-approved 3-6 months before buying

Pre-approval tells you exactly what you can borrow and your rate. This also reveals any credit issues with time to fix them before you find a home.

Should You Put 20% Down?

The traditional 20% down payment avoids PMI — but waiting to save 20% takes many years in expensive markets. Consider: a $350,000 home at 20% = $70,000 saved. At a 5% savings rate (HYSA), that's 10-12 years for many households.

Instead, many buyers put 5-10% down, pay PMI for 2-4 years, and request PMI cancellation once they reach 20% equity through payments and appreciation. On a $332,500 loan, PMI at 0.8% = $221/month. If putting 5% instead of 20% lets you buy 5 years sooner in an appreciating market, the PMI cost is often well worth it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do I need for a down payment? +
Minimums: VA = 0%, USDA = 0% (rural), FHA = 3.5%, Conventional = 3-20%. On a $350K home: 3% = $10,500, 5% = $17,500, 20% = $70,000. Also budget 2-4% for closing costs on top of your down payment.
Is it worth putting 20% down? +
20% eliminates PMI (saves $1,750-$5,250/year on a $350K loan), but takes years to save in most markets. Many buyers put 5-10% down and cancel PMI once they reach 20% equity. Buying sooner in an appreciating market often outweighs years of PMI payments.
Where should I keep my down payment savings? +
High-yield savings account (HYSA) or money market account earning 4-5% APY. Keep it out of the stock market — a downturn right before you buy could delay your purchase by years. For timelines under 3 years, capital preservation beats growth.
Can I use my Roth IRA for a down payment? +
Yes. First-time homebuyers can withdraw up to $10,000 in Roth IRA earnings penalty-free (contributions can always be withdrawn). Account must be 5+ years old. Weigh the long-term cost of reducing retirement savings before using this option.
What down payment assistance programs exist? +
Most states have HFA grants (free money, no repayment), forgivable loans, and deferred-payment programs. Income limits apply (typically 80-120% AMI). Search your state's housing finance authority or HUD.gov. Also look at Fannie Mae HomeReady and Freddie Mac Home Possible (3% down, reduced PMI).
