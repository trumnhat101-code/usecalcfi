APR vs APY: What's the Difference?

One rate costs you money. The other earns it. Here's exactly which number to look at — and when — for savings accounts, credit cards, and mortgages.
📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 📋 Sources: CFPB, Federal Reserve, Investopedia

Banks and lenders use APR and APY to describe interest — but they measure very different things. APR is what you pay when borrowing. APY is what you earn when saving. Mixing them up can lead to choosing the wrong credit card, the more expensive mortgage, or the lower-yielding savings account.

For Borrowing
APR
Annual Percentage Rate — the yearly cost of a loan including interest and most fees.
  • Mortgages
  • Credit cards
  • Auto & personal loans
  • Lower APR = cheaper loan
For Saving
APY
Annual Percentage Yield — the actual return after compounding is factored in.
  • Savings accounts
  • CDs & money market
  • Treasury bills
  • Higher APY = more earned

The Core Difference: Compounding

The key difference between APR and APY is compound interest. APR is a simple rate — it ignores how often interest compounds. APY bakes compounding in, so it shows your actual annual gain or cost.

APY FORMULA
APY = (1 + APR/n)n − 1
where n = compounding periods per year
Example — 5% APR with monthly compounding

APY = (1 + 0.05/12)12 − 1 = 5.116%
On $10,000: APR earns $500/year → APY earns $511.60/year. The $11.60 difference is compounding.

Stated Rate (APR)CompoundingActual APYDifference
5.00%Annually5.000%$0
5.00%Monthly5.116%+$11.60 on $10K
5.00%Daily5.127%+$12.70 on $10K
5.00%Continuously5.127%+$12.75 on $10K

The more frequently interest compounds, the larger the gap between APR and APY.

When APR Matters: Loans and Credit Cards

When you borrow, you pay APR. Use it to compare loan offers apples-to-apples. By law, lenders must disclose APR under the Truth in Lending Act (TILA).

Mortgage APR vs Interest Rate

Your mortgage rate is the base interest charge. APR is higher because it includes origination fees, discount points, and broker fees — spread over the loan term. Always compare mortgage offers by APR, not just rate.

Mortgage Example — $400,000 loan, 30 years

Lender A: 6.75% rate / 6.91% APR ($4,000 fees)
Lender B: 6.90% rate / 6.95% APR ($1,000 fees)
Lender A costs less overall despite the higher-fee loan, because its APR is lower.

When APY Matters: Savings and Investments

When you save, you earn APY. Federal law (Truth in Savings Act) requires banks to advertise savings rates as APY, so comparison is straightforward — the higher APY wins.

Why APY comparison matters on savings

$25,000 in a traditional savings at 0.50% APY earns $125/year.
$25,000 in a HYSA at 5.00% APY earns $1,250/year.
Same money. 10× the return. The only difference is where you keep it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is APR? +
APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. It includes the interest rate plus most fees, making it a more complete picture of loan cost than the interest rate alone. APR is used for mortgages, credit cards, and personal loans.
What is APY? +
APY (Annual Percentage Yield) reflects the actual return on a savings account or investment after accounting for compound interest. Unlike APR, APY shows what you actually earn when interest compounds monthly, daily, or continuously.
Which is higher — APR or APY? +
For the same stated rate, APY is always higher than or equal to APR because APY accounts for compounding. The more frequently interest compounds, the bigger the gap between APR and APY.
Why is my mortgage APR higher than the interest rate? +
Mortgage APR includes the interest rate plus origination fees, discount points, mortgage broker fees, and certain closing costs spread over the loan term. This makes APR a better comparison tool across different lender offers.
Should I compare savings accounts by APR or APY? +
Always compare savings accounts by APY. APY accounts for compounding frequency and shows your actual annual earnings. A savings account advertised at 5.00% APY pays more than one at 5.00% APR compounding monthly.
How do I convert APR to APY? +
Use the formula: APY = (1 + APR/n)^n − 1, where n is the number of compounding periods per year. For example, a 5% APR compounding monthly: APY = (1 + 0.05/12)^12 − 1 = 5.116%.
Does a higher APY always mean a better savings account? +
APY is the primary metric for comparing savings accounts, but also check minimum balance requirements, withdrawal restrictions, FDIC/NCUA insurance status, and whether the APY is promotional or ongoing. A high promotional APY that drops after 3 months may not be better than a slightly lower ongoing rate.
