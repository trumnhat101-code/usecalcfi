When Should You Take Social Security? 62 vs 67 vs 70

Deciding when to claim Social Security is one of the biggest financial decisions of your retirement. Claim at 62 and you start collecting immediately — but permanently at a reduced rate. Wait until 70 and your monthly check grows by up to 32% above your standard benefit. There is no universally correct answer. The right age depends on your health, other income, and break-even calculations.

📊 Data: Most Americans approach Social Security with far less saved than recommended. See Average Retirement Savings by Age 2026 for the real numbers.

This guide walks through everything you need to make an informed decision, backed by official Social Security Administration data.

How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated

Your Social Security benefit is based on your 35 highest-earning years, adjusted for wage inflation. The SSA calculates your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) — the monthly benefit you'd receive at your Full Retirement Age (FRA). Claiming before or after FRA adjusts that amount permanently, either up or down.

The key formulas (SSA official rates):

  • Early claiming (before FRA): Benefit is reduced 5/9 of 1% per month for the first 36 months early, then 5/12 of 1% per month beyond 36 months.
  • Delayed claiming (after FRA, up to 70): Benefit increases by 8% per year (2/3 of 1% per month).

Claiming at 62: Pros and Cons

Age 62 is the earliest you can claim Social Security. About 35% of Americans still claim at or near 62.

✅ Pros of claiming at 62

You start receiving income immediately. If you have health concerns or a shorter life expectancy, you collect more checks over your lifetime. Also useful if you have no other income and need the money now.

⚠️ Cons of claiming at 62

Your monthly benefit is permanently reduced by up to 30% (for FRA of 67). That reduction follows you for life, including future cost-of-living adjustments (COLA). If you continue working, the SSA withholds $1 for every $2 you earn above $22,320 (2026 limit) until you reach FRA.

Claiming at Full Retirement Age (66–67)

Claiming at your FRA gives you 100% of your calculated benefit with no reductions or withholding from earnings. For most people born after 1959, that's age 67.

💡 FRA is the neutral option

No reduction. No penalty for working. If you're in average health and unsure, FRA is a reasonable default — you collect your full calculated benefit without the risk of outlasting a reduced early benefit.

Claiming at 70: Maximum Benefit

Every month you delay past FRA, your benefit grows by 2/3 of 1% (8% per year). By age 70, the cumulative increase reaches:

  • FRA of 67: +24% vs FRA benefit (3 years × 8%)
  • FRA of 66: +32% vs FRA benefit (4 years × 8%)

There is no additional credit for waiting past 70. If you haven't claimed by 70, the SSA recommends filing then regardless.

✅ When waiting to 70 makes sense

Good health, family longevity, other income to bridge the gap, or you want to maximize survivor benefits for a spouse. The higher monthly amount also means larger COLA increases in future years.

⚠️ When waiting to 70 may not make sense

Poor health or shortened life expectancy. Urgent need for income. If the break-even age is beyond your realistic life expectancy, claiming earlier yields more total lifetime dollars.

The Break-Even Analysis

The break-even point is when cumulative lifetime benefits from waiting equals cumulative benefits from claiming early. If you live past the break-even age, waiting was worth it financially.

Special Situations to Consider

Spousal and Survivor Benefits

If you're married, your claiming decision also affects your spouse. A non-working or lower-earning spouse can claim up to 50% of your FRA benefit as a spousal benefit. More importantly, your spouse becomes eligible for your full benefit as a survivor benefit after you pass. The higher-earning spouse waiting until 70 can permanently boost the survivor benefit — a major advantage for couples.

Working While Claiming Before FRA

In 2026, if you claim before FRA and earn more than $22,320/year, the SSA withholds $1 for every $2 over the limit. In the year you reach FRA, the limit rises to $59,520 and withholding drops to $1 per $3 over that threshold. After you reach FRA, there is no earnings penalty.

Note: Withheld amounts are not lost — the SSA recalculates your benefit at FRA to credit months where benefits were withheld, slightly increasing your ongoing payment.

Tax Implications

Social Security benefits may be taxable depending on your combined income. If your combined income (adjusted gross income + nontaxable interest + half of SS benefits) exceeds $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married filing jointly), up to 50% of benefits are taxable. Above $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (joint), up to 85% is taxable. Claiming earlier may reduce taxation if you have lower overall income in those years.

Bottom Line: How to Decide

There is no perfect answer — it depends on your personal situation. Use these rules of thumb:

  • Claim at 62 if you have serious health concerns, no other income options, or a family history of shorter life expectancy.
  • Claim at FRA (66–67) if you're in average health, still working part-time, or want a safe middle ground without taking on longevity risk.
  • Wait to 70 if you're in good health, have other income to live on in the meantime, and want to maximize your monthly check and survivor benefits.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I take Social Security at 62?

Claiming at 62 permanently reduces your monthly benefit by up to 30% compared to your Full Retirement Age amount (for those born 1960 or later). The upside is you receive more total checks over your lifetime if your lifespan is shorter than average. The break-even against waiting to FRA is typically around age 79.

At what age do Social Security benefits max out?

Benefits stop growing at age 70. Delayed retirement credits accumulate at 8% per year from your FRA until 70. After that, there is no additional increase. For someone with FRA of 67, claiming at 70 yields a benefit 24% higher than the FRA amount.

Does working affect Social Security benefits before FRA?

Yes. If you claim before FRA and earn more than $22,320 in 2026, the SSA withholds $1 for every $2 earned above that limit. This withholding stops once you reach FRA, and withheld amounts are partially credited back through a slight benefit increase at FRA.

What is the break-even age for Social Security?

For most people comparing claiming at 62 vs FRA, the break-even age falls around 78–80. If you live past that age, waiting to FRA pays more in total lifetime benefits. Individual break-even varies based on your benefit amounts and the comparison ages you're evaluating.
