How Much Car Can You Afford? The 20/4/10 Rule (2026)

By Nathan Le

Quick answer: A safe guideline is the 20/4/10 rule — put at least 20% down, finance for no more than 4 years, and keep your total monthly vehicle cost (payment + insurance) under 10% of gross income. On a $60,000 salary (~$5,000/month), that's about a $26,000 car — roughly $500/month with a $5,200 down payment.

The car you want and the car you can afford are often two different numbers. Dealers quote you a monthly payment, stretch the loan to 6 or 7 years to make it look small, and suddenly you're paying for a car worth more than you owe. The 20/4/10 rule is a simple guardrail that keeps you out of that trap.

What Is the 20/4/10 Rule?

It's three limits that work together to keep a car purchase affordable:

  • 20% down — pay at least 20% of the price upfront (cash or trade-in).
  • 4-year loan — finance for 48 months or less.
  • 10% of income — keep your monthly payment (plus insurance) under 10% of your gross monthly income.
The rule in one line

20% down · ≤ 48-month loan · monthly cost ≤ 10% of gross monthly income

How Much Car Can You Afford by Salary?

Here's what the 10% payment cap supports at different incomes, assuming a 4-year loan at about 7% APR with 20% down. "Car price" is the total price you can buy, including your down payment.

$40,000$3,333~$333~$17,000
$60,000$5,000~$500~$26,000
$80,000$6,667~$667~$35,000
$100,000$8,333~$833~$43,000

Figures are estimates at ~7% APR over 48 months with 20% down. A higher rate, a shorter term, or adding insurance into the 10% lowers the price you can afford.

Why Each Part of the Rule Matters

20% down — so you're never "underwater"

New cars lose value fast — often 20% or more in the first year. A large down payment means your loan balance stays below the car's value, so you're not "upside-down" (owing more than it's worth). That protects you if the car is totaled or you need to sell early.

4-year loan — the sweet spot

Longer loans (6–7 years) shrink the monthly payment but cost far more in interest, and you stay underwater for years. A 4-year term keeps total interest reasonable and matches how fast the car depreciates. If you can't afford a car on a 4-year loan, it's usually a sign to choose a cheaper car — not a longer loan.

10% of income — so the car doesn't crowd out everything else

Transportation is one of the biggest household expenses. Capping the payment at 10% of gross income leaves room for rent, savings, and everything else. It also keeps your debt-to-income ratio healthy, which matters if you plan to apply for a mortgage.

Don't Forget the Total Cost of Ownership

The loan payment is only part of what a car costs. Budget for these too:

  • Insurance — the strict 20/4/10 rule includes this inside the 10%.
  • Fuel — varies with your commute and the vehicle's efficiency.
  • Maintenance & repairs — tires, oil, brakes, and the occasional big repair.
  • Registration, taxes, and fees — vary by state.
A safer full-budget target

Keep all transportation costs combined (payment + insurance + fuel + upkeep) under about 15–20% of your take-home pay. This lines up with the "needs" bucket of the 50/30/20 budget rule.

How to Find Your Own Number

  1. Start with gross monthly income. Not sure of your take-home vs gross? Use the paycheck calculator.
  2. Multiply by 10% to get your maximum monthly car cost.
  3. Subtract your estimated insurance to find how much is left for the loan payment.
  4. Work backward to a price. Plug a payment, rate, and 48-month term into the car payment calculator or the auto loan calculator to see the loan (and price) that fits.
  5. Add your 20% down to get the total car price you can comfortably afford.

When It's OK to Bend the Rule

The 20/4/10 rule is a guideline, not a law. Reasonable exceptions:

  • 0% or very low promotional financing — a longer term costs little extra interest, so a 5-year loan can be fine.
  • You pay cash — no loan means the 20%/4-year parts don't apply; just keep total ownership costs reasonable.
  • A reliable used car — buying used can put a solid car well within the 10% cap.

What you should almost never do is stretch to a 7-year loan on a car you can't otherwise afford. That's how people end up owing thousands on a car they no longer want.

This guide is for education and estimation only and is not financial advice. Loan terms, interest rates, and insurance costs vary by lender, credit profile, and state — confirm figures with your lender before buying.
