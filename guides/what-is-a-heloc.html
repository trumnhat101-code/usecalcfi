What Is a HELOC? How Home Equity Lines of Credit Work

By Nathan Le

HELOCs let you borrow against your home's equity like a credit card — but your house is the collateral. Here's how credit limits are set, how rates work, and when it actually makes sense to use one.

Quick answer: A HELOC (home equity line of credit) is a revolving credit line secured by your home's equity — you borrow as needed, like a credit card. Lenders typically let you borrow up to 80–85% of your home's value minus your mortgage balance, paying variable interest only on what you use.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📋 Sources: CFPB, Freddie Mac, Bankrate

What Is a HELOC?

A HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit) is a revolving line of credit secured by your home's equity. Think of it like a credit card — you have a credit limit, you borrow what you need, repay it, and borrow again. The key difference: your home backs the loan, which means lower interest rates but real foreclosure risk if you default.

How Your Credit Limit Is Calculated

Most lenders let you borrow up to 80–85% of your home's value minus your outstanding mortgage balance. Your combined loan-to-value (CLTV) ratio — your mortgage plus HELOC divided by home value — must typically stay at or below 85%.

HELOC LIMIT FORMULA
(Home Value × 0.80) − Mortgage Balance = HELOC Limit
Example — $400,000 Home

Home value: $400,000
Maximum borrowing (80%): $400,000 × 0.80 = $320,000
Minus mortgage balance: $320,000 − $250,000 = $70,000 HELOC limit

If home value rises to $450,000: limit increases to $450,000 × 0.80 − $250,000 = $110,000

Draw Period vs. Repayment Period

Phase 1
Draw Period
Typically 10 years. Borrow up to your limit, repay, borrow again. Payments are often interest-only — your principal can stay unchanged the entire period.
Phase 2
Repayment Period
Typically 20 years. No new draws. Full principal + interest payments required. Monthly payment can jump significantly if you drew heavily during Phase 1.

⚠️ Payment shock risk: If you borrowed $60,000 during the draw period making interest-only payments of ~$400/month, your repayment phase payment could jump to $500–$700/month including principal. Plan for this before drawing heavily.

HELOC Interest Rates

HELOC rates are variable — they move up and down with the prime rate, which is influenced by Federal Reserve policy. Your rate is typically: Prime Rate + Margin (margin varies by lender and credit score).

Some lenders offer a fixed-rate conversion option — you can lock a portion of your balance at a fixed rate. This protects against rate increases on the locked amount while keeping the rest flexible.

When to Use a HELOC (and When Not To)

✓ Good Uses
  • Home improvements that add value (kitchen remodel, addition, new roof)
  • Consolidating high-interest credit card debt (if disciplined)
  • Education expenses spread over time
  • Emergency backup fund for homeowners
  • Bridge financing between home sale and purchase
✗ Risky Uses
  • Vacations, dining, everyday expenses
  • Buying depreciating assets (cars, electronics)
  • Speculative investments or crypto
  • Funding a business with unstable income
  • Covering mortgage payments you can't afford

Your home is the collateral. If you default on a HELOC, the lender can foreclose — even if you're current on your primary mortgage.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a HELOC? +
A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by your home's equity. Like a credit card, you can borrow up to your limit, repay it, and borrow again during the draw period. The credit limit is based on your home's value minus your mortgage balance, typically up to 80-85% combined.
How is my HELOC credit limit calculated? +
Formula: (Home Value × 0.80) − Mortgage Balance = HELOC Limit. Example: $400,000 home × 80% = $320,000 minus a $250,000 mortgage = $70,000 available. Some lenders use 85% for well-qualified borrowers.
What is the difference between a HELOC and a home equity loan? +
A HELOC is a revolving line with a variable rate — you draw as needed. A home equity loan is a lump-sum loan with a fixed rate and predictable payments. HELOCs offer flexibility; home equity loans offer payment stability.
What are current HELOC interest rates? +
HELOC rates are variable, tied to the prime rate. In 2026, rates typically range from 7.5% to 10%+ depending on credit score and lender. Rates move up or down when the Federal Reserve changes its benchmark rate.
What can I use a HELOC for? +
Best uses: home improvements (especially value-adding projects), consolidating high-interest credit card debt, planned education expenses, and emergency reserves. Avoid using a HELOC for vacations, everyday spending, or speculative investments — your home is collateral.
What credit score do I need for a HELOC? +
Most lenders require 620–640 minimum. The best rates go to borrowers with 700+. You also typically need at least 15–20% home equity, a DTI under 43%, and stable income documentation.
