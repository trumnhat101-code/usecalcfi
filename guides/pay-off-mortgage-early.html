How to Pay Off Your Mortgage Early (And When Not To)

Paying off your mortgage early can save you tens of thousands of dollars in interest and give you the security of owning your home free and clear. On a $400,000 30-year loan at 7%, you'll pay over $558,000 in total interest if you stick to the minimum payment schedule. Early payoff strategies can slash that dramatically.

But early payoff isn't always the smartest move financially. This guide covers four proven strategies to pay off your mortgage ahead of schedule — and the situations where investing that money instead could be the better choice.

4 Proven Strategies to Pay Off Early

1

Make Extra Principal Payments Each Month

The simplest approach: add a fixed extra amount to every monthly payment, designated as "applied to principal." Even $100–200/month eliminates years and tens of thousands in interest. The key is consistency. Set it up as an automatic transfer or recurring payment so you don't think about it each month.

Important: Always specify "apply to principal" when submitting — otherwise some servicers apply it to next month's payment, which saves you no interest.

2

Switch to Biweekly Payments

Instead of 12 monthly payments per year, make half your monthly payment every two weeks. Because there are 52 weeks in a year, you end up making 26 half-payments — equivalent to 13 full monthly payments per year. That extra payment per year typically cuts a 30-year mortgage to about 25–26 years with no significant change to your monthly budget.

Many loan servicers offer a biweekly plan, sometimes for a small fee. Alternatively, you can replicate it by adding 1/12 of your monthly payment to each check.

3

Make a Lump-Sum Principal Payment

Apply a tax refund, bonus, inheritance, or windfall directly to your mortgage principal. Lump-sum payments are especially powerful early in the loan when more of each payment goes toward interest. A $10,000 lump sum on a $400,000 loan at year 2 eliminates roughly $25,000–30,000 in future interest over the life of the loan.

Apply windfalls early — the further you are into the amortization schedule, the less impact they have on interest savings.

4

Refinance to a Shorter Term

Refinancing from a 30-year to a 15-year mortgage cuts your total interest paid roughly in half — at the cost of a higher monthly payment. Rates on 15-year loans are typically 0.5–0.75% lower than 30-year rates, which increases the savings further.

The tradeoff: your required monthly payment increases by 30–50%, which reduces your financial flexibility. Only refinance to a shorter term if the higher payment is comfortably within your budget even in a downturn scenario.

Check for Prepayment Penalties First

Before making extra payments, check your loan documents for a prepayment penalty clause. Most conventional mortgages originated after 2014 cannot legally have prepayment penalties under the Dodd-Frank Act's Qualified Mortgage rules. But some older loans and certain adjustable-rate or portfolio mortgages still carry them — often 1–3% of the prepaid amount if you pay off more than 20% in a single year.

If you're unsure, call your loan servicer and ask directly: "Does my loan have a prepayment penalty?" Get the answer in writing before making any large principal payment.

⚠️ Also confirm how extra payments are applied

Some servicers automatically apply overpayments to "advance your payment due date" rather than reducing principal. When submitting an extra payment — online, by check, or by phone — always specify "Apply to principal balance only." Follow up with your next statement to verify it was applied correctly.

When Paying Off Your Mortgage Early Doesn't Make Sense

Early payoff is emotionally satisfying, but it's not always the highest-return use of extra cash. Here are situations where investing may be the smarter financial move:

Your mortgage rate is low (below 5%)

The S&P 500 has historically returned roughly 10% per year before inflation (~7% after inflation). If your mortgage rate is 4–5%, every extra dollar applied to your mortgage "earns" 4–5% guaranteed — but that same dollar invested in a diversified index fund has historically earned 7–10% per year. Over 20–30 years, the investment gap compounds significantly.

This comparison inverts when your rate is 6.5–7%+. At those rates, the guaranteed return of debt payoff competes much more favorably with stock market risk.

You haven't maxed out tax-advantaged accounts

Contributing to a 401(k) up to your employer match is an immediate 50–100% return — impossible to beat. Maxing out a Roth IRA ($7,000/year in 2026 for under 50) grows tax-free for decades. These should typically take priority over extra mortgage payments, especially in lower interest rate environments.

You don't have an emergency fund

If you don't have 3–6 months of expenses in liquid savings, redirecting cash to mortgage payoff leaves you financially vulnerable. One job loss or medical emergency could force you to take on high-interest debt to cover expenses — negating all the interest you saved.

✅ The recommended priority order

1. Emergency fund (3–6 months expenses)
2. 401(k) up to employer match (free money)
3. Pay off high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans >7%)
4. Max Roth IRA ($7,000/year)
5. Extra mortgage payments — especially if your rate is above 5.5–6%
6. Max 401(k) beyond the match

The Right Strategy for You

The best approach depends on your interest rate and financial situation. A simple rule of thumb:

  • Rate above 6.5%: Aggressively pay down your mortgage. The guaranteed return beats most alternatives adjusted for risk.
  • Rate 5–6.5%: Split the difference — put some extra toward the mortgage and some toward investments. Biweekly payments are an easy win with minimal budget impact.
  • Rate below 5%: Prioritize tax-advantaged investing. Let the inflation and investment returns work in your favor while the mortgage erodes in real terms.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do you save by paying extra on your mortgage?

On a $400,000 30-year mortgage at 7%, adding $300/month extra saves approximately $100,000 in interest and pays off the loan 8 years early. Even $100/month extra saves roughly $45,000 over the life of the loan. Use the Mortgage Payoff Calculator for your exact numbers.

Is it better to pay off my mortgage or invest?

If your mortgage rate is below 5–6%, investing in diversified index funds has historically produced higher returns. If your rate is above 6.5%, the guaranteed return of debt payoff is more competitive. You should also prioritize your emergency fund and tax-advantaged retirement accounts (401(k) match, Roth IRA) before extra mortgage payments.

Do extra mortgage payments go to principal?

They should — but you must designate them as "applied to principal" when submitting. Without that designation, some servicers apply overpayments to advance your next due date rather than reducing your balance. Always specify principal-only and verify it was applied correctly on your next statement.

What is a mortgage prepayment penalty?

A fee some lenders charge if you pay off a large portion of your loan early. Most conventional mortgages originated after 2014 cannot have prepayment penalties under Dodd-Frank QM rules. Older loans and some ARMs may still have them. Check your loan documents or ask your servicer before making large extra payments.
