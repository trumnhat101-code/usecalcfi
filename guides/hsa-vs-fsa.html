HSA vs FSA: Which Is Better?
2026 Limits & Rules

A complete comparison of Health Savings Accounts and Flexible Spending Accounts — 2026 contribution limits, rollover rules, eligible expenses, and which one saves you more.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: IRS Publication 969, IRS.gov

Both HSAs and FSAs let you pay healthcare expenses with pre-tax dollars — reducing your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. But they work very differently, and choosing the wrong one can mean losing money to the "use it or lose it" rule or missing out on one of the most powerful tax advantages in the tax code.

The HSA Triple Tax Advantage

🎯 Why the HSA Is Called "Triple Tax-Free"

1️⃣
Tax-Free Contributions
Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar
2️⃣
Tax-Free Growth
Investment gains, dividends, and interest grow without taxes
3️⃣
Tax-Free Withdrawals
Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are 100% tax-free

No other account in the U.S. tax code offers all three of these advantages simultaneously — not a 401(k), not an IRA, not a Roth IRA.

The HSA as a Stealth Retirement Account

Here's a strategy many financial advisors recommend: max out your HSA, pay medical bills out of pocket now, and save every receipt. The IRS has no time limit on when you can reimburse yourself for qualified medical expenses — meaning you can pay a $500 doctor bill today, save the receipt, and withdraw $500 from your HSA 20 years later completely tax-free.

Meanwhile, your HSA grows invested in mutual funds for 20 years. At age 65, your HSA becomes essentially a traditional IRA for non-medical expenses — you pay ordinary income tax on withdrawals (but no penalty). For medical expenses, it remains tax-free forever.

When to Choose FSA Instead

The FSA wins in these situations:

  • You have a low-deductible health plan — HSAs require an HDHP, so if your employer only offers PPO/HMO plans, FSA is your only option.
  • You have predictable, large medical expenses this year — FSA funds are available immediately on January 1, while HSA funds only become available as you contribute. If you need $3,000 for planned surgery in February, an FSA lets you use the full annual amount immediately.
  • You have dependents needing care — A Dependent Care FSA (for childcare/eldercare) is separate from a Health FSA and can be used alongside an HSA.
  • You're not disciplined about investing — If you'll spend the FSA money on healthcare anyway, the "use it or lose it" rule forces you to actually use the tax benefit.

Eligible Expenses (Both Accounts)

Both HSAs and FSAs cover the same broad range of qualified medical expenses:

  • Doctor visits, specialist copays, urgent care
  • Prescription medications
  • Dental care (cleanings, fillings, braces, implants)
  • Vision care (glasses, contacts, LASIK)
  • Mental health services (therapy, psychiatry)
  • Medical equipment (crutches, blood pressure monitors, CPAP machines)
  • Over-the-counter medications (since CARES Act 2020 — no prescription needed)
  • Feminine hygiene products (since CARES Act 2020)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between an HSA and FSA? +
An HSA requires a High-Deductible Health Plan and lets you roll over unused funds indefinitely — it's a triple-tax-advantaged investment account. An FSA works with most health plans but has a "use it or lose it" rule (with limited rollover). HSAs are generally superior for those eligible.
What are the 2026 HSA contribution limits? +
For 2026: $4,300 for self-only coverage and $8,550 for family coverage. Those age 55 or older can contribute an additional $1,000 catch-up contribution.
What are the 2026 FSA contribution limits? +
For 2026, the Health FSA contribution limit is $3,300 per employee. A married couple can each contribute up to $3,300 to their respective employer's FSA. The Dependent Care FSA limit is $5,000 per household.
Do HSA funds roll over? +
Yes. Unlike FSAs, HSA funds roll over year after year with no limit. Your HSA balance grows tax-free and can be invested in mutual funds. Many people use HSAs as a stealth retirement account — paying medical bills out of pocket now and reimbursing themselves decades later.
Can I have both an HSA and FSA? +
Generally no — you cannot have both a standard Health FSA and an HSA simultaneously. However, you can have an HSA alongside a Limited-Purpose FSA (LPFSA), which only covers dental and vision expenses. This lets you maximize both accounts.
What happens to my FSA if I change jobs? +
FSA funds are generally lost when you leave your employer, unless you elect COBRA continuation coverage. HSA funds are yours permanently — they stay with you regardless of job changes, insurance changes, or retirement.
