How to Lower Your DTI Ratio Fast (2026 Guide)

Lender DTI thresholds, what counts as debt, and 7 proven strategies to lower your debt-to-income ratio before your next loan application.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: Fannie Mae, FHA, CFPB

Your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio is one of the most important numbers lenders look at when you apply for a mortgage, auto loan, or personal loan. A DTI that's too high can mean rejection — or significantly worse terms. The good news: you can lower it strategically, even in a short window before applying.

How DTI Is Calculated

DTI = Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income × 100

Example: $3,250/month in debt payments ÷ $8,000 gross income = 40.6% DTI

Lenders use two versions: front-end DTI (housing costs only ÷ gross income) and back-end DTI (all monthly debt ÷ gross income). When people say "DTI," they usually mean back-end DTI.

What Counts as Debt in DTI?

Lenders count minimum monthly payments from your credit report:

  • Minimum credit card payments
  • Auto loan payments
  • Student loan payments (even if deferred — lenders often use 1% of balance or IBR payment)
  • Personal loan payments
  • Child support and alimony
  • The proposed new mortgage payment (for purchase loans)

Lenders do not count: utilities, cell phone, subscriptions, groceries, insurance, or other living expenses.

7 Strategies to Lower Your DTI

1

Pay Off Small Debts Entirely

Eliminating a payment removes the entire minimum from your DTI calculation. A $200/month car payment on a $3,000 balance is the fastest win — one payoff drops your DTI by $200/gross income.

Impact: 2-5% DTI reduction per eliminated debt
2

Pay Down High-Balance Credit Cards

Minimum payments on credit cards are typically 1-2% of balance. Reducing a $10,000 card to $2,000 drops the minimum from ~$200 to ~$40/month. Target cards closest to their limit first to also improve your credit score simultaneously.

Impact: 1-3% DTI reduction + credit score boost
3

Increase Your Income

DTI works on both sides of the equation. A $500/month side income drops DTI even without touching debt. Part-time work, freelancing, or a raise all count — but lenders typically need 2 years of self-employment income history.

Impact: $500 extra income on $8K gross = ~6% DTI reduction
4

Avoid Taking on New Debt

Every new loan or credit card application adds a potential new monthly payment to your DTI. No new car loan, furniture financing, or "buy now pay later" in the 6-12 months before a mortgage application.

Impact: Prevents DTI from increasing 2-8%
5

Refinance High-Payment Loans

Refinancing a personal loan or auto loan to a lower rate or longer term reduces the minimum monthly payment. Even if total interest is higher, a lower monthly payment directly reduces DTI. Only do this if you plan to apply for a mortgage soon.

Impact: 1-4% DTI reduction per refinanced loan
6

Switch to Income-Driven Repayment (Student Loans)

If on standard repayment, switching to an IBR or SAVE plan can dramatically lower your monthly student loan payment. Lenders use the actual payment on IBR plans. A $800/month standard payment that drops to $200/month on IBR reduces your DTI by $600/gross income.

Impact: Up to 8% DTI reduction for high student loan borrowers
7

Add a Co-Borrower

A co-borrower (spouse, partner, parent) adds their income to the denominator, potentially dropping your combined DTI significantly. Their debt payments also add to the numerator, so this helps most when the co-borrower has income but minimal debt.

Impact: Depends on co-borrower's income vs debt

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good DTI ratio? +
Below 36% is considered excellent by all lenders. Conventional mortgages prefer ≤43-45%. VA allows up to 41% (can exceed with residual income). FHA allows 43-57% with compensating factors. USDA requires ≤41%.
How is DTI ratio calculated? +
DTI = Total monthly debt payments ÷ Gross monthly income × 100. Example: $2,500 mortgage + $400 car + $200 student loan + $150 credit cards = $3,250 total. $3,250 ÷ $8,000 gross income = 40.6% DTI.
How fast can I lower my DTI? +
The fastest results come from paying off small debts entirely and switching student loan repayment plans. Both can take effect within 30-60 days. Increasing income also works immediately but lenders may require 2 years of history for self-employment income.
Does DTI include rent? +
Your current rent does NOT count in your DTI when applying for a mortgage — only minimum debt payments on credit-reported debts. However, the proposed new mortgage payment DOES count in the lender's DTI calculation.
Can I get a mortgage with 50% DTI? +
Yes, in some cases. FHA allows back-end DTI up to 57% with compensating factors (large down payment, high credit score, significant reserves). Some VA and conventional loans go to 50%+ with compensating factors. But high-DTI loans come with higher rates.
What counts as debt in DTI? +
Lenders count: minimum credit card payments, auto/student/personal loan payments, child support/alimony, other mortgage payments. They do NOT count: utilities, insurance, cell phone, subscriptions, groceries, or other living expenses.
