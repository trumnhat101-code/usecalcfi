How to Negotiate Your Salary: Scripts, Timing & Tactics (2026)

85% of people who negotiate get more. Here's exactly what to say — for job offers, raises, and everything in between.

Why Salary Negotiation Matters More Than You Think

Failing to negotiate even one job offer is one of the most expensive financial mistakes you'll make. A single missed negotiation at age 25 can compound into $500,000+ in lost lifetime earnings when you factor in annual raises, bonuses, and retirement contributions all building on a lower base.

💡 The Cost of Not Negotiating

Job offer: $75,000 vs. negotiated $82,000+$7,000/year
3% annual raises on higher base (10 years)+$22,000/year by year 10
Extra 401(k) contributions + match (10 years)~$35,000
10-year total difference~$115,000+
Most people don't negotiate: Only 39% of workers always negotiate salary. 85% of those who do get at least part of what they ask for. Employers budget negotiation room into initial offers.

Step 1: Research Your Market Value

Never negotiate without data. Walk in knowing the market rate for your role, location, experience level, and industry.

  1. Check multiple salary sourcesUse Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, Levels.fyi (tech), Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov), and PayScale. Cross-reference at least 3 sources. Look at role + city + years of experience.
  2. Talk to people in the fieldRecruiters will tell you market rates — they want to place you. Former colleagues, LinkedIn connections, or industry groups can give real numbers.
  3. Know your total compensation, not just salaryBase salary + bonus + equity + 401(k) match + benefits. A $90K job with a 10% bonus and 5% 401(k) match is worth more than a $95K job with none of those.

How to Negotiate a Job Offer

Timing: When to Bring Up Salary

  • During interviews: Deflect if possible. "I'm flexible — I'd love to understand the full role and comp structure first."
  • After the offer: This is your power moment. You have leverage once they've decided they want you.
  • Never accept immediately: Always ask for 24–48 hours to review: "Thank you! I'm excited. Can I have until tomorrow to review the full offer?"

The Negotiation Script (Job Offer)

📞 Phone or in-person:
"Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this role and the team. I've done some research on the market rate for this position in [city], and based on my [X years of experience / specific skill], I was hoping we could get closer to [your number — 10-15% above target]. Is there flexibility there?"

⏸️ Then: stay silent. Let them respond first.
📧 Email version:
"Thank you for the offer — I'm very enthusiastic about joining the team. After reviewing the details, I'd love to discuss the base salary. Based on my research into the market rate for this role in [city], and given my background in [key skill], I was expecting something in the range of [X to Y]. Is there room to work with that?"

Anchoring: Always Go First With a Specific Number

Research shows the first number stated becomes the anchor that shapes the negotiation. Name a specific salary (not a range) that is 10–15% above your actual target:

Your TargetFirst Ask (Anchor)Likely Outcome
$80,000$88,000–$92,000$82,000–$87,000
$100,000$110,000–$115,000$103,000–$108,000
$130,000$145,000–$150,000$133,000–$140,000
Never give a range. If you say "$80,000–$90,000," they hear "$80,000." Always give a single specific number — it signals confidence and research.

How to Negotiate a Raise

Best Times to Ask for a Raise

  • After completing a major project or hitting a big goal
  • During annual review cycle — before budgets are finalized
  • After taking on significantly more responsibility
  • When you have a competing offer (but only use this if you're willing to leave)

The Raise Script

📅 Request the meeting:
"I'd love to schedule time to discuss my compensation. I've been tracking my contributions and want to make sure we're aligned on my career path here."

💬 In the meeting:
"Over the past [time period], I've [specific accomplishments: led X project, grew Y by Z%, took on A responsibility]. Based on my research, the market rate for this role at my level in [city] is [range]. I'd like to discuss increasing my base to [specific number]."

Negotiation Tactics That Actually Work

The Silence Tactic
After stating your number, stop talking. Silence is uncomfortable — they'll often fill it with a concession.
Specific Numbers
Ask for $97,500, not $100,000. Odd numbers suggest careful research and feel more credible.
The Flinch
When they give a number, pause, look slightly pained, say "Hmm..." This signals you expected more without saying it.
Bundle Don't Cave
If they won't budge on salary, ask for a signing bonus, extra PTO, flexible schedule, or earlier review date simultaneously.
Get It in Writing
Always confirm any verbal offer via email immediately: "Just to confirm, the agreed base is $X starting [date]..." Prevents walkbacks.
The Competing Offer
A real competing offer is your most powerful leverage. Don't bluff — use it only if you'd actually take the other job.

Common Salary Negotiation Mistakes

  • Accepting the first offer immediately — Shows you didn't research the market and leaves money on the table
  • Apologizing for negotiating — "Sorry to ask, but..." weakens your position before you've started
  • Giving a low anchor — If you anchor too low, you cap your outcome before negotiating begins
  • Negotiating over email when possible to do by phone — Voice communication is harder to ignore or lowball
  • Revealing your current salary — In many states, employers legally cannot ask. You don't have to answer.
  • Burning the relationship — Stay collaborative, not adversarial. Frame it as solving a problem together.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it okay to negotiate salary?
Yes. Studies show 85% of people who negotiate get at least some of what they ask for. Most hiring managers expect negotiation — only 39% of candidates actually do it, which means negotiators earn more than peers doing identical work.
How much should I ask for when negotiating salary?
Ask for 10-20% above your actual target. This gives room to settle at your goal. Always anchor higher than you need — you can always come down, never up. Use a specific number, not a range.
What should I say when negotiating salary?
A simple script: "I'm very excited about this opportunity. Based on my research and experience, I was expecting something closer to [X]. Is there flexibility there?" Then stay silent and wait for their response.
When is the best time to negotiate salary?
For job offers, negotiate after you receive the written offer — not during the interview. For raises, best timing is right after a major win or during the annual review cycle, before budgets are finalized.
What if they say no to my salary request?
Ask about non-salary compensation: signing bonus, extra vacation days, remote work, earlier review date, or professional development budget. If truly stuck, ask: "When can we revisit this after 90 days?"
