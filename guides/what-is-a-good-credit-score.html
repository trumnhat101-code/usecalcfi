What Is a Good Credit Score?
FICO Ranges Explained (2026)

By Nathan Le

A complete breakdown of FICO score ranges, what each tier means for your loan rates, and exactly how to improve your score — backed by CFPB data.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: FICO, CFPB, Experian

Your credit score is a three-digit number — typically between 300 and 850 — that lenders use to evaluate how likely you are to repay debt. The most widely used scoring model is the FICO Score, used in 90% of lending decisions in the U.S. A higher score unlocks better interest rates, higher credit limits, and easier loan approvals.

The short answer: a score of 670 or above is "good." But the real money is made at 740+, where lenders offer their best rates.

How Your Credit Score Affects Mortgage Rates

Your credit score has a direct, measurable impact on the interest rate you're offered. On a $350,000 30-year mortgage, the difference between a 620 and a 760 score can cost — or save — over $80,000 in interest over the life of the loan.

Credit utilization (amounts owed ÷ total credit limit) is the fastest lever you can pull. Experts recommend keeping utilization below 30%, ideally below 10%, for the best score impact. Paying down a maxed-out card can boost your score by 20–50 points within one billing cycle.

7 Proven Ways to Improve Your Credit Score

  • Pay every bill on time. Payment history is 35% of your score. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every account. Even one missed payment can drop your score 50–100 points.
  • Pay down credit card balances. Get your utilization below 30% on every card. Below 10% is ideal. This is the single fastest way to improve your score.
  • Don't close old accounts. Closing a credit card reduces your total available credit and shortens your credit history — both hurt your score. Keep old cards open, even if you don't use them.
  • Limit hard inquiries. Applying for new credit triggers a hard inquiry that can drop your score 5–10 points. Avoid applying for multiple cards or loans in a short period.
  • Dispute errors on your credit report. 1 in 5 Americans has an error on their credit report. Get your free report at AnnualCreditReport.com and dispute any inaccuracies — errors can be removed within 30 days.
  • Become an authorized user. Ask a family member with excellent credit to add you as an authorized user on their account. Their payment history and credit limit help boost your score.
  • Diversify your credit mix. Having a mix of revolving credit (cards) and installment loans (auto, student) shows lenders you can manage different debt types. Don't take out debt just for this — but if you need a loan, it can help.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is considered a good credit score? +
A FICO score of 670–739 is considered "Good." A score of 740–799 is "Very Good," and 800+ is "Exceptional." Most lenders offer their best rates to borrowers with scores of 740 or higher. A score of 670+ will typically qualify you for most loans, though not always at the best rates.
What credit score do I need to buy a house? +
Most conventional mortgages require a minimum credit score of 620. FHA loans allow scores as low as 580 (with 3.5% down). However, to get the best mortgage rates, you generally want a score of 740 or higher. Each 20-point increase in your score can save thousands over the life of a loan.
How long does it take to improve a credit score? +
Small improvements can happen in 1–2 months by paying down credit card balances or getting errors removed. Larger improvements — like recovering from a late payment — typically take 6–12 months of consistent on-time payments. Recovering from major events like bankruptcy can take 7–10 years, though the impact diminishes over time.
What are the five factors that make up a credit score? +
FICO scores are calculated from: Payment History (35%), Amounts Owed / Utilization (30%), Length of Credit History (15%), New Credit (10%), and Credit Mix (10%).
Does checking my own credit score hurt it? +
No. Checking your own credit score is a "soft inquiry" and has no impact on your score. Only "hard inquiries" — when lenders check your credit for a loan application — can temporarily lower your score by a few points.
What is the average American credit score? +
The average FICO score in the United States is approximately 718, which falls in the "Good" range. About 58% of Americans have a FICO score of 700 or higher.
