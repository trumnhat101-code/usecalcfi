What Is a Good DTI Ratio? Lender Standards Explained (2026)

Quick answer: A good debt-to-income (DTI) ratio is 36% or below, which lenders consider ideal. Most mortgages allow a total DTI up to 43%, and some up to 45–50% with strong credit or a large down payment. The lower your DTI, the better your interest rate and approval odds.

Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is one of the most important numbers lenders look at when you apply for a mortgage, auto loan, or personal loan. It tells lenders how much of your income is already spoken for by existing debt. Get it right and loan approval becomes much easier — and you pay less interest.

What Is DTI and How Is It Calculated?

DTI is calculated by dividing your total monthly debt payments by your gross monthly income (before taxes):

DTI Formula

DTI = Total Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income × 100

Example: $2,000 in monthly debt payments ÷ $6,000 gross income = 33% DTI

Monthly debt payments include: mortgage or rent, car loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments, personal loans, and any other recurring debt obligations. They do not include utilities, groceries, insurance premiums, or discretionary spending.

📊 Data: Credit card debt is one of the biggest drivers of high DTI. See Average Credit Card Debt by State 2026 to compare your state's burden.

DTI Ranges: What They Mean

Below 36% — Excellent
Lenders consider this low-risk. You'll qualify for the best rates on most loan types.
36–43% — Good / Manageable
Most lenders approve this range. You qualify for conventional mortgages and most loan types.
44–50% — High / Borderline
Approval becomes lender-dependent. FHA and VA may still approve; conventional lenders may require compensating factors.
Above 50% — Risky
Most lenders will decline. You'll likely need to pay down debt before qualifying for a major loan.

Mortgage DTI Requirements by Loan Type

Lenders often look at two DTI numbers for mortgages: the front-end ratio (housing costs only ÷ income) and the back-end ratio (all debt ÷ income). The back-end is the more important number.

💡 Compensating factors

Lenders can approve higher DTIs when you have compensating factors: a large down payment (20%+), significant cash reserves, a high credit score (740+), or stable long-term employment. Even at 47–50% DTI, strong compensating factors can get you approved for an FHA loan.

DTI Standards for Other Loan Types

Auto Loans

Auto lenders are more flexible than mortgage lenders. Most prefer a total DTI below 50%, but many will approve loans at higher ratios — at higher interest rates. A DTI below 36% typically gets you the best auto loan rates.

Personal Loans

Personal lenders (banks, credit unions, online lenders) typically want a total DTI below 40–45%. Some online lenders accept up to 50%, but expect rates above 20% APR. Your credit score matters just as much as DTI for personal loans.

Credit Cards

Credit card issuers don't use DTI as formally, but they do consider your total debt obligations relative to income. Carrying balances above 30% of your credit limit (credit utilization) can harm your credit score and indirectly affect DTI-sensitive decisions.

How to Lower Your DTI Ratio

There are only two levers: reduce your debt or increase your income. Here's how to do both:

Reduce Monthly Debt Payments

  • Pay off high-minimum debts first. Credit cards with even small balances carry minimum payments of 1–3% of the balance. Paying off a $5,000 card eliminates ~$100–150/month in minimum payments.
  • Refinance student loans or auto loans to a lower monthly payment by extending the term (though this increases total interest paid).
  • Avoid new debt in the months before a major loan application. New car loans, furniture financing, and store cards all increase your monthly obligations.
  • Consolidate debt. Rolling multiple high-payment debts into one lower-rate personal loan can reduce total monthly minimums.

Increase Gross Income

  • Ask for a raise or negotiate a salary increase before applying for a mortgage — even a modest bump in documented income significantly moves the DTI needle.
  • Add a part-time job or freelance income (must typically be documented for 2 years for mortgage purposes).
  • Include all eligible income sources: rental income, Social Security, alimony, investment income — as long as it's consistent and documentable.
⚠️ Don't quit your job before closing on a mortgage

Lenders verify employment and income right before closing. Changing jobs — even to a higher-paying one — can delay or derail approval if the new income can't be verified immediately.

DTI vs Credit Score: Which Matters More?

Both matter, but they measure different things. Your credit score reflects how reliably you've repaid past debt. Your DTI reflects how much room you have in your budget for new debt. A good credit score with a high DTI can still get you declined. A lower credit score with a strong DTI can sometimes still get you approved — especially for FHA loans which accept scores as low as 580 at 3.5% down.

For mortgage approval, most lenders consider DTI the harder constraint: you can compensate for a mediocre credit score, but you cannot override a DTI above 50% at most institutions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good DTI ratio for a mortgage?

For a conventional mortgage, most lenders prefer a total back-end DTI of 43% or lower. FHA allows up to 43–50% with strong compensating factors. VA loans use a 41% guideline but may approve higher with sufficient residual income.

What DTI is too high to get a loan?

A total DTI above 50% makes approval very difficult for most loan types. Most mortgage programs cap at 43–50%. Above that threshold, you'll typically need to reduce debt or increase income before applying.

Does DTI include rent or mortgage?

For your current DTI calculation, yes — if you have a current rent or mortgage payment, that's included in your monthly debt total. When you apply for a new mortgage, lenders replace your current housing cost with the proposed new payment to calculate your expected post-loan DTI.

How do I lower my DTI ratio quickly?

The fastest method is to pay off small-balance, high-minimum-payment debts (like credit cards) in full. Even eliminating $3,000 in credit card debt can remove $90–150/month in minimums, meaningfully reducing your DTI. Avoid taking on any new debt before your loan application.
