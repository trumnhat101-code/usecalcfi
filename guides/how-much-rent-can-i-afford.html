How Much Rent Can You Afford? The 30% Rule (2026)

By Nathan Le

Quick answer: The 30% rule says to keep rent under 30% of your gross monthly income. On a $60,000 salary (~$5,000/month), that's about $1,500/month in rent. Many landlords also require your gross income to be at least 3× the rent — the same math from the other direction.

Rent is usually the biggest line in a monthly budget, so getting the number right matters. Spend too much and there's nothing left for savings, debt, or emergencies. The 30% rule is a simple starting point that has guided renters for decades — here's how to use it, and when to bend it.

What Is the 30% Rule?

The rule is one line: rent should be no more than 30% of your gross (pre-tax) monthly income. Landlords use the same threshold in reverse — most require that your gross monthly income be at least three times the rent, which works out to rent being about 33% of income.

The rule in one line

Monthly rent ≤ 30% of gross monthly income  ·  equivalently, income ≥ 3× rent

How Much Rent Can You Afford by Salary?

Here's what 30% of gross income looks like at common salaries:

Annual salaryGross / monthMax rent (30%)
$40,000$3,333~$1,000
$50,000$4,167~$1,250
$60,000$5,000~$1,500
$75,000$6,250~$1,875
$100,000$8,333~$2,500

These use gross income. Not sure of your gross vs take-home? Check the paycheck calculator.

Gross vs Net: Which Should You Use?

The 30% rule and landlord "3× rent" requirements both use gross (pre-tax) income. That's fine for qualifying — but you actually pay rent out of your take-home pay. If your taxes or retirement contributions are high, 30% of gross can feel tight in practice.

A good sanity check: make sure rent stays under about 40% of your take-home pay. If it doesn't, look for a cheaper option before signing.

When the 30% Rule Doesn't Work

In expensive metros, spending exactly 30% on rent is often impossible. That doesn't make the rule useless — it just means you need a plan for the gap:

  • Get a roommate. Splitting a 2-bedroom is the fastest way to bring your share back under 30%.
  • Trade space or location. A smaller unit or a neighborhood one stop further out can save hundreds a month.
  • Accept a higher ratio, then cut elsewhere. If rent has to be 35–45%, keep total needs under about 50% of take-home pay using the 50/30/20 budget rule — that means trimming wants to stay balanced.

Don't Forget the Full Cost of Renting

Rent isn't your only housing cost. Budget for these too:

  • Utilities — electricity, gas, water, trash, internet (some are included, some aren't).
  • Renter's insurance — usually $10–25/month and often required by the landlord.
  • Upfront costs — security deposit, first (and sometimes last) month, and application or pet fees.
  • Parking — a separate monthly charge in many buildings.
A safer full-budget target

Keep all housing costs (rent + utilities + renter's insurance) under about 35% of gross income, and total "needs" under 50% of take-home — the "needs" bucket of the 50/30/20 rule.

How to Find Your Own Number

  1. Find your gross monthly income (annual salary ÷ 12). Use the paycheck calculator to see gross vs take-home.
  2. Multiply by 30% to get your rent ceiling.
  3. Subtract expected utilities and renter's insurance to see what's realistic.
  4. Plug it into a budget. The budget calculator shows how rent fits alongside savings and other needs, and our 50/30/20 budget tracker lets you plan it out month by month.
