First-Time Homebuyer Guide 2026:
Everything You Need to Know

By Nathan Le

Step-by-step: from checking your credit score to getting keys in hand. Loan types, down payment programs, closing costs, and what to watch out for.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 10 min read 📚 Sources: HUD, Fannie Mae, FHA, VA

Buying your first home is the largest financial decision most Americans ever make. The process can feel overwhelming — but it follows a predictable set of steps. This guide walks you through each one, from checking your credit to sitting at the closing table.

Who Counts as a "First-Time Homebuyer"?

Most loan programs define a first-time homebuyer as someone who has not owned a primary residence in the past 3 years. Even if you owned a home before, you may still qualify. Single parents who previously co-owned with a spouse and certain other situations also qualify under HUD's broader definition.

The 8-Step Homebuying Process

1

Check & Improve Your Credit Score

Pull your free credit reports at AnnualCreditReport.com. Dispute errors. Pay down credit card balances below 30% utilization. Minimum scores: FHA = 580, Conventional = 620, VA/USDA = typically 640+. Best rates require 740+.

2

Determine Your Budget

Most lenders use the 28/36 rule: housing costs ≤28% of gross income, total debt ≤36%. Use our Home Affordability Calculator to find your number. Don't forget property tax, insurance, HOA, and maintenance (budget 1-2% of home value/year).

3

Save Your Down Payment + Closing Costs

Minimum down payment depends on loan type (see below). Also budget 2-5% of loan amount for closing costs. For a $350,000 home with 5% down: need ~$17,500 down + up to $16,600 in closing costs = ~$34,000 total to bring to closing.

4

Get Pre-Approved

Pre-approval (not just pre-qualification) is a real credit check that gives you a commitment letter. Shop at least 3 lenders — comparing rates within 14-45 days counts as one hard inquiry. A 1% lower rate on $350K saves ~$65,000 over 30 years.

5

Find a Real Estate Agent & Start Shopping

A buyer's agent costs you nothing (seller pays). Use an agent who specializes in first-time buyers. Make a non-negotiables list (location, bedrooms, commute radius) vs nice-to-haves before you start touring.

6

Make an Offer & Negotiate

Your agent handles the offer paperwork. Include contingencies: inspection, financing, and appraisal. Earnest money (1-3% of price) is due within 1-3 days — it goes toward your down payment at closing.

7

Home Inspection & Appraisal

Inspection (~$400-600) protects you from hidden problems. Appraisal is required by the lender to confirm the home is worth what you're paying. If the appraisal comes in low, you can renegotiate the price, pay the difference, or walk away.

8

Final Walk-Through & Closing

Walk through the home 24-48 hours before closing to verify agreed repairs were made. At closing, you sign ~100 pages of documents, pay closing costs, and receive keys. Closing typically takes 1-2 hours.

Loan Types for First-Time Buyers

VA Loan

0% down
  • Veterans & active duty only
  • No PMI ever
  • Best rates available
  • VA funding fee (can waive if disabled)

USDA Loan

0% down
  • Rural/suburban areas only
  • Income limits apply
  • No PMI (upfront guarantee fee)
  • Check USDA eligibility map

FHA Loan

3.5% down
  • 580+ credit score
  • MIP for life of loan
  • Easier to qualify
  • Good if credit is 580-679

Conventional

3–20% down
  • 620+ credit score minimum
  • PMI cancels at 20% equity
  • Best if credit 680+
  • Lower total cost than FHA long-term

Common First-Time Buyer Mistakes to Avoid

  • Shopping for homes before getting pre-approved. Sellers won't consider offers without a pre-approval letter.
  • Opening new credit or making large purchases during the process. This can change your DTI and tank your loan approval days before closing.
  • Draining savings to make a larger down payment. Keep 3-6 months of mortgage payments in reserve. Lenders often want to see cash reserves.
  • Skipping the inspection. Never waive the inspection to compete in a hot market — you could inherit $30,000+ in hidden repairs.
  • Overbuying on house. Mortgage approval isn't the same as affordability. Factor in utilities, maintenance, HOA, and any planned life changes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do you need to buy a house? +
Minimum scores: FHA = 580 (3.5% down) or 500 (10% down). Conventional = 620+, best rates at 740+. VA has no VA minimum but lenders typically require 620+. USDA typically 640+. A higher score saves significant money — at $350K, the difference between 620 and 760 can be $200+/month.
How much do you need for a down payment? +
VA loan = 0%, USDA = 0% (rural, income limits), FHA = 3.5% (580+ score), Conventional = 3-5% (some programs) or 20% to avoid PMI. Down payment assistance programs exist in most states to help with this.
What is the first-time homebuyer definition? +
For most programs: you have not owned a primary residence in the past 3 years. So even prior homeowners can qualify. HUD has a broader definition for some programs including single parents who previously co-owned.
What are closing costs? +
Fees paid at settlement, typically 2-4% of the loan amount. On a $332,500 loan, expect $6,650–$13,300. Includes lender fees, appraisal, title insurance, prepaid taxes and insurance. You can negotiate seller concessions to cover some.
How long does the homebuying process take? +
Pre-approval: 1-5 days. House hunting: weeks to months. Once under contract, closing: 30-45 days (conventional), 30-60 days (FHA/VA). Total process from starting search to closing keys: typically 2-6 months for first-time buyers.
What is PMI and can I avoid it? +
PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) is required on conventional loans with less than 20% down. It costs 0.5–1.5% of your loan annually. Avoid it with: VA loan (no PMI ever), 20% down, 80-10-10 piggyback loan, or some credit union programs. It cancels when your balance reaches 78% of original purchase price.
