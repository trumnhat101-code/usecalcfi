How Much Should You Have Saved for Retirement by Age?

One of the most common retirement planning questions is: Am I on track? The answer depends on your income, lifestyle, and goals — but benchmark targets give you a useful starting point. This guide covers the most widely used retirement savings benchmarks by age, the math behind them, and what to do if you're behind.

The Standard Retirement Savings Benchmarks

Fidelity Investments — one of the largest retirement account managers in the US — publishes savings benchmarks based on decades of data. These assume you retire at 67, maintain your current lifestyle, and have Social Security supplementing your savings. The benchmarks are multiples of your current annual salary:

By Age 30

1× your annual salary

Example: Earning $60,000 → Target: $60,000 saved

This is achievable starting at 22–23 with 10–15% contribution rate. At 30, the exact balance matters less than your contribution rate — time and compound growth are your biggest assets at this stage.

By Age 40

3× your annual salary

Example: Earning $80,000 → Target: $240,000 saved

This is where many people fall short. Career transitions, kids, mortgages, and lifestyle inflation in your 30s often crowd out retirement contributions. If you're at 2× rather than 3×, focus on maximizing your 401k and opening a Roth IRA.

By Age 50

6× your annual salary

Example: Earning $100,000 → Target: $600,000 saved

Your 50s are your highest-earning years and your last decade of serious compounding. At 50, catch-up contributions kick in: you can contribute $31,000/year to a 401k ($23,500 + $7,500 catch-up) and $8,000 to an IRA ($7,000 + $1,000 catch-up) in 2026.

By Age 60

8× your annual salary

Example: Earning $110,000 → Target: $880,000 saved

At 60, you're 5–7 years from retirement. Shift focus to: finalizing your Social Security strategy, reducing portfolio risk, modeling healthcare costs (Medicare starts at 65), and calculating your specific retirement income needs.

The Math Behind These Benchmarks

These targets are based on a 15% savings rate (including employer match), 5.5% average annual real return, and retiring at 67 with Social Security covering roughly 40% of pre-retirement income. The goal is to replace 45% of your pre-retirement income from savings, using the 4% safe withdrawal rate.

Under the 4% rule, $800,000 generates $32,000/year in retirement income. If Social Security provides $20,000–$25,000/year, your total retirement income on $800,000 saved is roughly $52,000–$57,000 — close to 70% of the $80,000 pre-retirement salary in this example.

💡 The power of starting early: a $10,000 example

$10,000 invested at age 25 at 7% annual return = ~$150,000 by age 65. The same $10,000 invested at age 45 = ~$39,000 by age 65. Starting 20 years earlier multiplies the outcome by 3.9× on the same initial investment.

What If You're Behind?

Most Americans are behind these benchmarks. According to the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances, the median retirement savings for Americans 45–54 is around $134,000 — far below the 4–6× salary targets for that age. If that's you, here's what to do:

If you're 30–40 and behind

  • Increase your contribution rate by 1% per year until you reach 15%. Small increases are barely noticeable in take-home pay but compound significantly over decades.
  • Always capture the full employer match — this is an immediate 50–100% return. Never leave it on the table.
  • Open a Roth IRA if you don't have one. $7,000/year in a Roth grows tax-free. At 7% for 30 years, that's $700,000+ of tax-free retirement income from $210,000 of contributions.
  • Automate contributions. Pay yourself first before any discretionary spending reaches your bank account.

If you're 40–55 and significantly behind

  • Maximize catch-up contributions from age 50: $31,000/year to a 401k is $15,500 more than the base limit. Maximizing for 15 years at 6% return adds $360,000 to your balance.
  • Delay retirement by 2–3 years if possible. Each year of delay means one fewer year of withdrawal and one more year of contributions and growth. It also increases your Social Security benefit by 6–8%.
  • Reduce lifestyle inflation. The gap between income and spending is your savings rate. At this stage, cutting expenses has more impact than chasing investment returns.
  • Consider a paid-off home as part of the plan. Entering retirement with no mortgage significantly reduces your income needs.

If you're 55–65 and behind

  • Max all tax-advantaged accounts: $31,000 (401k) + $8,000 (IRA) + $5,300 (HSA if eligible) = $44,300/year
  • Consider working 1–2 years longer — the combined effect of additional contributions, portfolio growth, and higher Social Security benefits is significant
  • Build a detailed retirement income plan: Social Security timing, required expenses, Medicare costs, and how long your savings need to last

How Much Do You Need to Retire? The 4% Rule

The 4% rule (also called the "safe withdrawal rate") is a widely accepted guideline from the Trinity Study. It states that you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one of retirement, then adjust for inflation annually, and have a high probability of not running out of money over a 30-year retirement.

To use it: estimate your annual retirement spending, subtract Social Security income, and multiply the remainder by 25. That's your target portfolio.

⚠️ Limitations of the 4% rule

The 4% rule was designed for a 30-year retirement. If you retire at 55, you may need a 3–3.5% withdrawal rate. It also assumes a diversified portfolio and doesn't account for healthcare cost spikes or unusually high inflation periods. Use it as a starting point, not an exact prescription.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I have saved for retirement at 40?

The standard benchmark is 3× your annual salary. So if you earn $70,000, your target is $210,000 by 40. Many people fall short of this due to spending pressures in their 30s — if that's you, focus on maximizing contributions now rather than dwelling on the gap. Use the 401k calculator to model how much you can add by retirement at your current rate.

Is $1 million enough to retire on?

For many Americans, yes — especially with Social Security. Using the 4% rule, $1 million generates $40,000/year. With $20,000–$25,000 from Social Security, a couple can live on $60,000–$65,000/year, which is above median household income. For high earners or expensive-cost-of-living areas, $1.5–$2.5 million is more appropriate.

What percentage of my salary should I save for retirement?

The widely cited target is 15% of gross income, including employer match. If you started at 25 and your employer matches 3%, you need to contribute 12% to hit 15% total. If you started later, 20%+ is appropriate. The minimum floor is whatever captures your full employer match.

How do I catch up on retirement savings in my 50s?

Take full advantage of catch-up contributions: $31,000/year to your 401k and $8,000 to an IRA for a total of $39,000/year in tax-advantaged savings. Consider delaying Social Security — waiting from 62 to 67 increases your monthly benefit by up to 30%, and waiting to 70 adds another 24% on top of that. Even 2–3 extra years of work can dramatically improve your retirement picture.
