What Is PMI and How
to Avoid It? (2026)

Private mortgage insurance explained: what it costs, when lenders require it, and 5 strategies to avoid paying it — potentially saving you hundreds of dollars every month.

Quick answer: PMI (private mortgage insurance) is a fee lenders add to conventional loans when your down payment is under 20%. It typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount per year — about $40–$125 per month for every $100,000 borrowed — and automatically cancels once you reach 20% equity.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 6 min read 📚 Sources: CFPB, Fannie Mae, HUD

PMI — private mortgage insurance — is one of the least understood costs of homeownership. It protects your lender if you default, yet you pay for it. On a $400,000 home with 5% down, PMI can add $150–$400 to your monthly payment. Understanding PMI and how to eliminate it can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of your loan.

What Is PMI?

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is a type of insurance policy required by most conventional mortgage lenders when a borrower puts down less than 20% of the home's purchase price. The policy protects the lender — not you — in case you stop making payments. Despite this, you pay the premium.

PMI is not the same as homeowner's insurance (which protects your property) or life insurance. It's purely a lender protection mechanism that you fund until you've built enough equity in your home.

5 Ways to Avoid PMI

  • Put 20% down. The simplest way — save until you can put 20% down and PMI never applies. Use our Down Payment Calculator to plan your savings timeline.
  • Use a piggyback loan (80-10-10). Take an 80% first mortgage, a 10% second mortgage (home equity loan), and put 10% down. No single loan exceeds 80% LTV, so no PMI. The second loan typically carries a higher rate, so compare total costs carefully.
  • Get a lender-paid PMI (LPMI) loan. The lender pays your PMI in exchange for a slightly higher interest rate. No monthly PMI line item, but you pay more interest over the life of the loan. Best if you plan to sell or refinance within a few years.
  • Use a VA loan. VA loans (for eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and some surviving spouses) never require PMI regardless of down payment — one of the VA loan's biggest advantages.
  • Look for no-PMI loan programs. Some lenders offer conventional loans with less than 20% down and no PMI, especially for borrowers with excellent credit (760+) or certain professions (doctors, lawyers). Shop around — not all lenders offer these programs.

How to Cancel PMI You're Already Paying

✅ Three Ways PMI Ends

1. Automatic cancellation at 78% LTV: Under the federal Homeowners Protection Act (HPA), your lender must automatically cancel PMI when your loan balance drops to 78% of the original purchase price — assuming you're current on payments. This happens through your regular monthly payments.

2. Request cancellation at 80% LTV: Once you reach 80% LTV (20% equity), you can proactively request PMI cancellation in writing. The lender may require a home appraisal to confirm value hasn't declined. This can happen faster than automatic cancellation if your home has appreciated.

3. Refinance your mortgage: If your home has appreciated significantly, refinancing to a new loan at 80% or lower LTV eliminates PMI. Compare refinance costs against PMI savings to determine if it's worth it — use our Refinance Calculator to run the numbers.

If you're choosing between FHA and conventional: if your credit score is 680+, a conventional loan with PMI is often cheaper than an FHA loan with MIP — especially because FHA MIP may never cancel, while PMI does.

Is PMI Always Bad?

Not necessarily. PMI allows you to buy a home now with a smaller down payment rather than waiting years to save 20%. In markets where home prices are rising, buying sooner — even with PMI — can mean significant equity gains that outweigh the PMI cost. Frequently Asked Questions

What is PMI (private mortgage insurance)? +
PMI stands for Private Mortgage Insurance. It protects the lender if you default on your loan. Lenders typically require it when you put down less than 20% on a conventional mortgage. It costs 0.5%–1.5% of your loan amount per year, added to your monthly payment.
How much does PMI cost? +
PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of your original loan amount per year. On a $350,000 loan, that's roughly $146–$438 per month. Your exact rate depends on your credit score, down payment percentage, and loan type.
When does PMI go away automatically? +
Under the Homeowners Protection Act, your lender must automatically cancel PMI when your loan balance reaches 78% of the original purchase price — as long as you're current on payments. At 80% LTV, you can request cancellation.
Can I avoid PMI with less than 20% down? +
Yes. A piggyback loan (80-10-10) lets you put 10% down while avoiding PMI. Some lenders offer "no-PMI" loans that fold PMI into a slightly higher interest rate. VA loans never require PMI regardless of down payment.
Is PMI tax deductible? +
PMI deductibility has varied by year — Congress has periodically extended this deduction. Check with a tax professional or IRS.gov for the current year's rules.
Does FHA require mortgage insurance? +
FHA loans require Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP), similar to PMI but with different rules. FHA MIP includes an upfront premium (1.75% of loan amount) plus annual premiums. Unlike PMI, FHA MIP may be required for the life of the loan if you put down less than 10%.
