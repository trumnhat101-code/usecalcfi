🌎 Why Do Some States Have So Much More Debt?

The gap between D.C. ($8,620) and Mississippi ($5,420) is $3,200 — nearly 60% more debt. Several factors explain this:

  • Cost of living: Residents in high-cost states like Connecticut, New Jersey, and Hawaii often rely on credit to cover everyday expenses that exceed income in lower-cost states.
  • Income and credit limits: Higher incomes qualify residents for higher credit limits, which correlates with higher balances — even if the debt-to-income ratio is similar.
  • Financial sophistication and credit access: Urban, high-income areas have greater access to premium credit cards, which often carry higher credit limits.
  • Housing costs: States with very high rent or mortgage payments leave less discretionary income, pushing more daily spending onto credit cards.

🧰 4 Proven Strategies to Pay Off Credit Card Debt Faster

1. Avalanche Method (Saves the Most Interest)

List all credit card balances from highest APR to lowest. Pay minimums on everything, then throw all extra money at the highest-rate card. Once it's paid off, roll that payment to the next card. This method minimizes total interest paid.

2. Snowball Method (Best for Motivation)

List balances from smallest to largest. Pay minimums everywhere, then attack the smallest balance first. The psychological win of eliminating cards keeps people motivated. Costs slightly more in interest than avalanche, but many people stick with it longer.

3. Balance Transfer to 0% APR Card

Many cards offer 0% APR for 12–21 months on balance transfers (typical fee: 3–5%). If you can realistically pay off most of your balance in that window, this can save hundreds or thousands in interest. Warning: don't accumulate new charges on the old card.

4. Increase Your Payment — Even a Little

As shown in the table above, adding just $50/month to your payment can cut years off your payoff timeline. Automate a slightly higher payment so you don't have to think about it.

Avoid these traps: Making only minimum payments, closing paid-off cards (it can hurt your credit score), and continuing to use high-balance cards while trying to pay them down.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average credit card debt in the United States?
The average American credit card balance in 2026 is approximately $6,730, based on Federal Reserve G.19 consumer credit data and Experian's State of Credit report. Total revolving credit card debt in the U.S. has surpassed $1.17 trillion.
Which state has the highest average credit card debt?
The District of Columbia has the highest average credit card balance at $8,620, followed by Alaska ($8,350) and Connecticut ($7,940). These areas tend to have higher costs of living and higher incomes, which correlate with higher credit limits and balances.
Which state has the lowest average credit card debt?
Mississippi has the lowest average credit card balance at $5,420, followed by Wisconsin ($5,560) and Idaho ($5,500). Midwestern and Southern states generally have lower balances, often correlated with lower costs of living and more conservative spending habits.
How long does it take to pay off average credit card debt?
At the national average balance of $6,730 with a 22.8% APR and a fixed $200/month payment, it takes approximately 4.5 years to pay off the balance, with about $4,110 in total interest paid. Paying $300/month reduces this to about 2.6 years and saves over $1,600 in interest.
How much does the average American pay in credit card interest per year?
At the national average balance of $6,730 and the current average APR of 22.8%, the average American with credit card debt pays approximately $1,534 per year in interest charges alone — or about $128 per month. This is money that provides no benefit and builds no equity.
What is the current average credit card APR?
As of early 2026, the average credit card APR is approximately 22.8% according to the Federal Reserve's G.19 statistical release. This is near historic highs following the rate cycle of 2022–2024, making it more expensive than ever to carry a revolving balance.

📐 Methodology & Data Sources

  • Average balance data: State-level figures are derived from Experian's State of Credit 2024 report, adjusted with Federal Reserve G.19 consumer credit trends through Q4 2025 and projected to 2026. Figures represent average balances among cardholders carrying a balance (not all credit card holders).
  • APR: 22.8% is the Federal Reserve's reported average APR on credit card accounts assessed interest as of Q4 2025 (Series G.19).
  • Payoff calculations: Assumes a fixed monthly payment of $200 (not decreasing minimum payments). Formula: n = -ln(1 - r × P / PMT) / ln(1 + r), where r = monthly rate (22.8%/12), P = balance, PMT = monthly payment.
  • Annual interest: Calculated as average balance × 22.8% APR. Actual interest varies based on payment timing and daily periodic rate application.
  • National average: $6,730 represents an approximate population-weighted average of state-level figures, consistent with Federal Reserve aggregate data.
  • Total U.S. credit card debt: $1.17 trillion sourced from Federal Reserve Z.1 Financial Accounts of the United States, Q4 2025 estimate.

Sources: Federal Reserve G.19 Consumer Credit Statistical Release • Experian State of Credit 2024 • Federal Reserve Z.1 Financial Accounts • CFPB Consumer Credit Trends
