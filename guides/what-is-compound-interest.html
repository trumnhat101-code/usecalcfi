What Is Compound Interest?
How It Works & Why It Matters

The complete guide to compound interest — the formula, the Rule of 72, how $10,000 grows over 40 years, and how to harness compounding to build long-term wealth.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📚 Sources: Federal Reserve, SEC Investor Education

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world" — and while the attribution is disputed, the math isn't. Compound interest is the engine behind long-term wealth building, and also the force that keeps millions of Americans trapped in credit card debt. Understanding it is non-negotiable for your financial health.

Simple Interest vs Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. If you deposit $10,000 at 7% simple interest for 10 years, you earn $700/year × 10 years = $7,000 in interest. Final balance: $17,000.

Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus all previously earned interest. At 7% compounded annually for 10 years, your $10,000 becomes $19,672 — not $17,000. That extra $2,672 comes from earning interest on your interest.

Over 30 years, the same $10,000 at 7% simple interest = $31,000. At 7% compound interest = $76,123. The difference is dramatic — and it gets more dramatic with time.

The Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)nt
A = Final amount
P = Principal (starting amount)
r = Annual interest rate (as decimal: 7% = 0.07)
n = Compounding periods per year (12 = monthly)
t = Time in years

Example: $10,000 invested at 7% compounded monthly for 20 years:
A = 10,000 × (1 + 0.07/12)(12×20) = $40,079

Emma contributes $5,000/year for just 10 years and then stops. Larry contributes $5,000/year for 30 years and never stops. Yet Emma ends up with more money at retirement — purely because of compounding time. This is the single most powerful lesson in personal finance.

Compound Interest Working Against You

The same math that builds wealth can destroy it on the debt side. Credit cards typically charge 20–30% APR compounded monthly. A $5,000 credit card balance at 24% APR, making only minimum payments, can take 17+ years to pay off and cost over $9,000 in interest — nearly double the original balance.

This is why paying off high-interest debt before investing is almost always the right move. Guaranteed "returns" of 20–30% (by eliminating interest charges) beat most investment returns. See our Debt Avalanche vs Snowball guide for the fastest payoff strategy.

How to Make Compound Interest Work for You

  1. Start as early as possible. Time is the most powerful ingredient. Even small amounts invested in your 20s outperform larger amounts invested later.
  2. Reinvest dividends and returns. Don't take dividends in cash — reinvest them to compound your growth.
  3. Use tax-advantaged accounts. 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, and HSA accounts let compound interest grow without annual tax drag — dramatically accelerating growth.
  4. Increase contributions over time. As your income grows, increase what you invest. Even $100/month more can mean $200,000+ more at retirement.
  5. Minimize fees. A 1% annual fee on a $500,000 portfolio costs $5,000/year — but also reduces compounding. Over 20 years, 1% in fees can cost you 20%+ of your final wealth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is compound interest? +
Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest (which only applies to the original principal), compound interest causes your money to grow exponentially — because you earn interest on your interest.
What is the formula for compound interest? +
A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A = final amount, P = principal, r = annual interest rate (decimal), n = compounding periods per year, t = time in years. Example: $10,000 at 7% annual compounded annually for 10 years: A = 10,000(1.07)^10 = $19,672.
What is the Rule of 72? +
Divide 72 by your annual interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money. At 7%: 72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3 years. At 10%: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years. At 4%: 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years. It also works for debt — a 20% credit card doubles your balance in 3.6 years.
How often should interest compound? +
More frequent compounding means slightly more growth. Order from least to most: annually, semi-annually, quarterly, monthly, daily. For most savings accounts, daily compounding is standard. The difference between monthly and daily compounding is small, but the difference between annual and monthly compounding is meaningful over decades.
Why does starting early matter so much? +
Starting 10 years earlier can more than double your final wealth. A 25-year-old who invests $5,000/year for 10 years then stops ends up with more at 65 than a 35-year-old who invests $5,000/year for 30 years — at 7% returns. This is the power of early compounding.
How does compound interest work against you? +
Compound interest works the same way on debt. A credit card at 20% APR compounds monthly — a $5,000 balance with minimum payments can take 15+ years and cost over $8,000 in interest. High-interest debt should be your first financial priority.
