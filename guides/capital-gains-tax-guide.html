Capital Gains Tax Guide 2026: Rates, Rules & How to Reduce It

Quick answer: Long-term capital gains — on assets held more than a year — are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your taxable income. Short-term gains, on assets held a year or less, are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%. Holding investments longer almost always lowers your tax.

Selling stocks, a home, or investments? Here's exactly how capital gains taxes work in 2026 — and legal ways to pay less.

What Is a Capital Gain?

A capital gain is the profit you make when you sell an asset for more than you paid for it. The cost basis is what you paid (including commissions). The gain is the sale price minus your cost basis.

Capital Gain = Sale Price − Cost Basis

Example: Bought 100 shares of stock at $50 = $5,000 basis
Sold at $80/share = $8,000 sale price
Capital Gain = $8,000 − $5,000 = $3,000

Short-Term vs Long-Term: The Critical Difference

The single most important factor in how much tax you pay is how long you held the asset.

⚡ Short-Term Capital Gains

  • Assets held 1 year or less
  • Taxed as ordinary income
  • Rates: 10% to 37%
  • Same as your regular tax bracket
  • Most expensive — avoid when possible

✅ Long-Term Capital Gains

  • Assets held more than 1 year
  • Taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, 20%
  • Much lower than ordinary income rates
  • The most powerful single tax move: hold 1+ year

Capital Gains on Home Sales

Selling your home? You may qualify for a big exclusion. If you owned and lived in the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years:

Filing StatusExclusion Amount
SingleUp to $250,000 of gain excluded
Married Filing JointlyUp to $500,000 of gain excluded
Example: Bought home for $300,000, sold for $700,000
Gain = $400,000
Married exclusion = $500,000
Taxable gain = $0 — no capital gains tax owed

If your gain exceeds the exclusion, only the amount above the exclusion is taxed at long-term rates (assuming 2+ years ownership).

Special Capital Gains Situations

Inherited Assets — Stepped-Up Basis

When you inherit an asset, your cost basis is stepped up to the fair market value on the date of the original owner's death. This means you only owe capital gains on appreciation after you inherited it — not the entire history of the asset.

Gifted Assets

If you receive a gift, you generally take the donor's original cost basis. If you sell at a gain, you owe capital gains tax based on that original basis. Exception: if the fair market value at the time of the gift was lower than the donor's basis, special rules apply.

Collectibles (Art, Coins, Antiques)

Long-term gains on collectibles are taxed at a maximum rate of 28% — higher than stocks and real estate.

Real Estate Depreciation Recapture

If you sell rental property, you must pay a maximum 25% tax on any depreciation you previously claimed as deductions. This is called "unrecaptured Section 1250 gain."

7 Legal Ways to Reduce Capital Gains Tax

📅
Hold 1+ Year
The simplest strategy. Wait until assets qualify for long-term rates. Can cut your rate from 37% to 0–20%.
📉
Tax-Loss Harvesting
Sell losing investments to offset gains. You can deduct up to $3,000 of net losses against ordinary income annually, carrying the rest forward.
🌱
Invest in Roth IRA / 401(k)
Gains inside tax-advantaged accounts are never subject to capital gains tax. Roth: 100% tax-free. 401(k): tax-deferred until withdrawal.
🎁
Give Appreciated Stock to Charity
Donate appreciated stock directly (not cash) to charity. You get a full deduction for FMV and never pay capital gains on the appreciation.
👶
Gift to Low-Income Family Members
If family members are in the 0% long-term rate bracket (income under ~$47K), they can sell your gifted appreciated stock tax-free.
🏘️
Use 1031 Exchange (Real Estate)
Defer capital gains on investment property by rolling proceeds into a "like-kind" property within 45 days of sale.
📊
Time Gains in Low-Income Years
If you expect a lower-income year (career change, early retirement), sell appreciated assets then to hit the 0% bracket.

Tax-Loss Harvesting Example

You have: $15,000 gain on Stock A
You have: $8,000 unrealized loss on Stock B

Sell Stock B → realize the $8,000 loss
Net taxable gain: $15,000 − $8,000 = $7,000
At 15% rate: pay $1,050 instead of $2,250
Tax saved: $1,200
Wash-sale rule: You cannot buy the "same or substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after selling for a loss. Violating this disallows the loss deduction.

How to Report Capital Gains

Capital gains are reported on Schedule D of your Form 1040. Your brokerage will send a Form 1099-B each year showing your sales, proceeds, and cost basis. Key deadlines:

  • Tax return due: April 15 (or extension to October 15)
  • If you expect to owe $1,000+ in tax: pay estimated taxes quarterly to avoid underpayment penalties
  • Quarterly due dates: April 15, June 16, September 15, January 15
Good news for low earners: If your total taxable income (including the capital gain) stays below $47,025 (single) or $94,050 (married), your long-term capital gains rate is 0%. Strategic early retirees use this to harvest gains completely tax-free.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the capital gains tax rate for 2026?
Long-term capital gains are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. Short-term gains (held under 1 year) are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%.
How do I avoid capital gains tax on stocks?
Hold stocks for over 1 year to qualify for lower long-term rates. Invest through Roth IRAs or 401(k)s for tax-free or tax-deferred growth. Use tax-loss harvesting to offset gains with losses.
Do I have to pay capital gains tax on my home sale?
If you owned and lived in the home for 2 of the last 5 years, you can exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 married filing jointly) from taxes.
What is the capital gains tax on inherited property?
Inherited assets receive a stepped-up basis to fair market value at the date of death. You only pay capital gains on appreciation after you inherited it — often resulting in zero tax if sold quickly.
What is the net investment income tax (NIIT)?
High earners (income over $200K single / $250K married) pay an additional 3.8% NIIT on top of capital gains tax. This makes the effective top rate 23.8% on long-term gains.
