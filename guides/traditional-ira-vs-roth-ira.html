Traditional IRA vs Roth IRA:
Which Is Better for You?

A side-by-side comparison of contribution limits, tax rules, income thresholds, and which account wins for your situation — updated for 2026.

Quick answer: Choose a Traditional IRA if you want a tax deduction now and expect a lower tax rate in retirement; choose a Roth IRA if you'd rather pay tax now and withdraw tax-free later. Both let you contribute up to $7,000 in 2026 ($8,000 if 50+). Roth has income limits; Traditional doesn't.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 📚 Sources: IRS Publication 590-A, IRS.gov

Both Traditional and Roth IRAs are tax-advantaged retirement accounts — but they're taxed at opposite ends of the timeline. Understanding which one benefits you more comes down to one question: will your tax rate be higher today or in retirement?

Earn too much for a Roth? High earners can use the backdoor Roth IRA strategy: contribute to a non-deductible Traditional IRA, then immediately convert it to a Roth. This is legal and widely used.

Which Should You Choose?

🏦 Choose Traditional IRA if…

  • You're in a high tax bracket now (32%+)
  • You expect significantly lower income in retirement
  • You need the tax deduction to reduce this year's tax bill
  • You're over 50 and want to maximize pre-tax savings quickly
  • Your state has high income taxes now but you'll retire in a no-tax state

💜 Choose Roth IRA if…

  • You're early in your career (lower tax bracket now)
  • You expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement
  • You want tax-free income in retirement
  • You want to avoid Required Minimum Distributions
  • You want the flexibility to withdraw contributions penalty-free
  • You're using it as an estate planning tool for heirs

If your tax rate stays the same or goes up in retirement, the Roth wins. If your tax rate drops significantly, the Traditional may come out ahead. Most young Americans are better served by the Roth because their highest earning — and taxing — years are still ahead of them.

Can You Have Both?

Yes — and many people do. You can contribute to both a Traditional and Roth IRA in the same year, as long as your total combined contributions don't exceed $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). This is called "splitting" your IRA contributions and can be a good hedging strategy against future tax uncertainty.

What About a 401(k)?

IRAs are separate from 401(k) plans. If your employer offers a 401(k) match, always contribute enough to get the full match first — that's an immediate 50–100% return. After capturing the match, redirect additional savings to your IRA. Many employers now also offer Roth 401(k) options, which combine the higher contribution limits of a 401(k) with the tax-free growth of a Roth.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I choose a Traditional IRA or Roth IRA? +
Choose a Roth IRA if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement than you are now. Choose a Traditional IRA if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement. If you're early in your career or in a low tax bracket today, the Roth IRA is almost always the better choice.
Can I contribute to both a Traditional and Roth IRA? +
Yes, you can contribute to both in the same year, but your total combined contributions cannot exceed the annual limit — $7,000 in 2026 ($8,000 if age 50 or older).
What are the Roth IRA income limits for 2026? +
For 2026, Roth IRA contributions phase out between $150,000–$165,000 for single filers and $236,000–$246,000 for married filing jointly. Above these limits you cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA, but you can use the backdoor Roth strategy.
Does a Traditional IRA require minimum distributions? +
Yes. Traditional IRAs require Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) starting at age 73. Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime, which makes them powerful estate planning tools for passing wealth to heirs.
Can I convert a Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA? +
Yes, through a Roth conversion. You'll owe income tax on the converted amount in the year of conversion, but the funds then grow tax-free. This strategy (sometimes called a backdoor Roth) is popular for high earners who exceed Roth income limits.
What happens if I withdraw from an IRA before age 59½? +
For Traditional IRAs, early withdrawals are subject to income tax plus a 10% penalty (with some exceptions). For Roth IRAs, you can withdraw your contributions (not earnings) at any time without tax or penalty, since you already paid tax on those funds.
