Self-Employed Taxes Guide 2026

The 15.3% self-employment tax, quarterly payment deadlines, every major deduction you're entitled to, the QBI deduction, and retirement accounts that slash your tax bill.
📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 9 min read 📋 Sources: IRS Publication 334, 505, 560

The Self-Employment Tax (SE Tax)

When you're employed, your employer pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes. When you're self-employed, you pay both halves — that's the 15.3% self-employment tax. The good news: you can deduct half of it to reduce your taxable income.

Social Security
12.4%
On net earnings up to $176,100 (2026 wage base)
Medicare
2.9%
No income cap. +0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above $200K single/$250K married
HOW SE TAX IS CALCULATED
Net Profit × 0.9235 × 0.153 = Self-Employment Tax
The 0.9235 factor accounts for the employer-equivalent deduction
Example — $80,000 Net Profit

$80,000 × 0.9235 = $73,880 (SE taxable earnings)
$73,880 × 0.153 = $11,304 self-employment tax
Deduct half ($5,652) from adjusted gross income → reduces taxable income
In the 22% bracket, that deduction saves you ~$1,243 in income tax

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Unlike employees who have taxes withheld from each paycheck, self-employed individuals pay taxes quarterly. If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal taxes for the year, you must make estimated payments — or face underpayment penalties.

How Much to Pay Each Quarter

The simplest approach: Safe Harbor Method — pay 100% of last year's total tax liability in four equal installments (or 110% if prior year AGI exceeded $150,000). This guarantees no underpayment penalty regardless of how your current-year income turns out.

Alternatively, estimate current-year income, subtract deductions and half the SE tax, apply your tax bracket, and divide by 4. Pay via IRS Direct Pay or Electronic Federal Tax Payment System (EFTPS).

⚠️ Set aside 25–30% of every payment you receive. A common rule of thumb for self-employed taxpayers in the 22% bracket: set aside 15% for SE tax + 10% for federal income tax + state tax. Automate this transfer to a separate savings account on every receipt so you're never caught short at quarterly deadlines.

Top Schedule C Deductions

Self-employed individuals deduct business expenses on Schedule C, reducing net profit before SE tax is calculated. Every dollar of deduction saves you roughly 15.3 cents in SE tax plus your marginal income tax rate.

🏠 Home Office
Exclusive business-use area only. Simplified: $5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max). Regular method: percentage of home expenses (rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance).
🚗 Vehicle
Standard mileage: $0.70/mile (2026 rate) for business miles. Or actual expenses × business-use %. Keep a mileage log — the IRS often audits this.
🏥 Health Insurance
100% of premiums for yourself, spouse, and dependents deductible above the line — not on Schedule C, but as a personal deduction that reduces AGI.
💻 Equipment & Software
Computers, phones, cameras, software subscriptions used for business. Section 179 lets you deduct full cost in the year of purchase instead of depreciating.
🍽 Business Meals
50% of business meals with clients or for business travel. Document the business purpose, attendees, and date. Personal meals are not deductible.
📚 Education & Training
Courses, books, seminars that maintain or improve skills in your current business. Education to start a new career or meet minimum requirements is not deductible.
👔 Professional Services
Accountant fees, attorney fees, business consulting fees paid for your business. Deduct the year you pay them.
📦 Advertising & Marketing
Website hosting, domain fees, social media ads, business cards, SEO tools, and any marketing expense directly related to your business.

The QBI Deduction (20% Off Your Taxes)

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction under Section 199A lets most self-employed people deduct up to 20% of qualified business income from their taxable income — with no cash outlay required.

QBI Deduction Example

Net profit after expenses: $80,000
Minus half SE tax deduction: −$5,652 → Adjusted QBI: $74,348
QBI deduction (20%): $14,870
In the 22% bracket, this deduction saves you $3,271 in income tax

Filing StatusFull QBI DeductionPhase-Out Range
Single / Head of HouseholdUnder $197,300$197,300 – $247,300

Specified Service Trade or Businesses (law, financial services, consulting, health) face additional restrictions above the phase-out thresholds. General businesses (retail, manufacturing, real estate) are not restricted.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is the self-employment tax rate? +
The SE tax is 15.3% — 12.4% Social Security (on net earnings up to $176,100) and 2.9% Medicare (no cap). You deduct half (7.65%) as an above-the-line deduction, reducing your adjusted gross income. Higher earners also pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax above $200K (single).
When are quarterly estimated taxes due? +
Due four times per year: April 15 (Q1), June 16 (Q2), September 15 (Q3), and January 15 of the next year (Q4). If you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes, you must make estimated payments to avoid underpayment penalties.
What can I deduct as self-employed? +
Major deductions include: home office (exclusive business use), vehicle (standard mileage $0.70/mile in 2026), health insurance premiums, retirement contributions, business equipment and software, professional services, 50% of business meals, advertising, and business-related education.
What is the QBI deduction for self-employed? +
The QBI deduction lets most self-employed people deduct up to 20% of qualified business income. For 2026, it fully applies for single filers with income under $197,300 and joint filers under $394,600, with phase-outs above those thresholds for some service businesses.
How do I calculate my self-employment tax? +
Multiply net profit by 0.9235, then by 0.153. Example: $80,000 × 0.9235 = $73,880 × 0.153 = $11,304 SE tax. Deduct half ($5,652) from your gross income as an above-the-line deduction to reduce taxable income.
What is the best retirement account for self-employed? +
The Solo 401k has the highest limits ($70,000 total for 2026) and allows Roth contributions — best for high earners who want to maximize tax-advantaged savings. The SEP-IRA is simpler to set up (25% of net earnings, max $70,000) but lacks Roth options and catch-up contributions.
