Roth IRA Rules and Contribution Limits 2026

2026 limits, income phase-outs, the 5-year rule, and how high earners can still use a Roth through the backdoor strategy.

Quick answer: For 2026, you can contribute up to $7,000 to a Roth IRA, or $8,000 if you're 50 or older. Contributions are made with after-tax dollars and grow tax-free, with qualified withdrawals in retirement entirely tax-free. Eligibility phases out at higher incomes.

📅 Updated June 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📋 Sources: IRS, Fidelity, Vanguard

2026 Contribution Limits

Roth IRA limits are set annually by the IRS. For 2026, the limits are unchanged from 2025. You can only contribute up to your earned income for the year (wages, self-employment income) — not investment income.

Under Age 50
$7,000
Per year max contribution
Age 50 and Older
$8,000
Includes $1,000 catch-up contribution

These limits are shared across all your IRAs. If you have both a Traditional and a Roth IRA, the combined contributions cannot exceed $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). The limit applies per person, not per account.

⚠️ Tax filing deadline = contribution deadline. You can make 2026 Roth IRA contributions until April 15, 2027 (the 2026 tax filing deadline). Contributing in January gives your money more time to compound versus waiting until April.

Income Limits (MAGI Phase-Out Ranges)

Roth IRA eligibility phases out at higher incomes. Use your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) — not your gross salary — to determine how much you can contribute.

Single / Head of HouseholdUnder $150,000$150,000 – $165,000$165,000+
Married Filing JointlyUnder $236,000$236,000 – $246,000$246,000+

During the phase-out range, your contribution limit is reduced proportionally. For example, a single filer with $157,500 MAGI (halfway through the $15,000 phase-out) can contribute $3,500 — half the normal limit.

The 5-Year Rule Explained

The Roth IRA 5-year rule is one of the most misunderstood retirement rules. It determines when your earnings can be withdrawn tax-free.

The 5-Year Rule

Your Roth IRA must be open for at least 5 years AND you must be age 59½ or older before earnings can be withdrawn completely tax and penalty-free (a "qualified distribution").

The 5-year clock starts January 1 of the year you make your first contribution — even if that contribution was made December 31. A 2026 contribution starts the clock on January 1, 2026, so the 5 years end December 31, 2030.

What You Can Always Withdraw (No 5-Year Rule)

Your contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn at any time, for any reason, tax and penalty-free. You already paid taxes on this money. The 5-year rule only applies to earnings.

*Exceptions to the 10% penalty include first home purchase (up to $10,000), disability, qualified education, and substantially equal periodic payments.

Backdoor Roth IRA (for High Earners)

If your income exceeds the phase-out range, you can still fund a Roth IRA using the backdoor strategy. There is no income limit on Roth conversions — only on direct contributions.

Contribute to a non-deductible Traditional IRA

Anyone with earned income can contribute up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) to a Traditional IRA — regardless of income. The contribution is non-deductible (after-tax).

File IRS Form 8606

This records your after-tax (non-deductible) basis in the Traditional IRA. Critical for avoiding double taxation when you convert.

Convert to Roth IRA

Convert the Traditional IRA funds to your Roth IRA. If converted quickly after contribution (before earnings accumulate), there is minimal or no tax owed.

⚠️ Pro-Rata Rule: If you have other pre-tax IRA money (rollover IRA, SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA), the IRS taxes conversions proportionally across all your IRAs — not just the non-deductible amount. Consider rolling pre-tax IRAs into your employer 401k first to avoid this complication.

Roth IRA vs Traditional IRA

Traditional IRA
  • Contributions may be tax-deductible
  • Grows tax-deferred
  • Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
  • Required Minimum Distributions at 73
  • No income limit to contribute
  • Better if tax rate is higher now than in retirement
Roth IRA
  • Contributions are after-tax (no deduction)
  • Grows completely tax-free
  • Qualified withdrawals 100% tax-free
  • No RMDs during your lifetime
  • Income limits apply for direct contributions
  • Better if tax rate is lower now than in retirement

Not sure which to pick? Many financial advisors suggest contributing to both — a Traditional 401k at work and a Roth IRA — to have tax diversification in retirement.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit? +
The 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000 for individuals under age 50, and $8,000 for those 50 and older. You cannot contribute more than your earned income for the year.
What is the income limit for a Roth IRA in 2026? +
Single filers can contribute the full amount with MAGI under $150,000, phasing out at $165,000. Married filing jointly can contribute fully under $236,000, phasing out at $246,000. Above those limits, use the backdoor Roth strategy.
What is the Roth IRA 5-year rule? +
Your Roth IRA must be open for at least 5 years before earnings can be withdrawn tax-free. The clock starts January 1 of the year of your first contribution. Contributions (not earnings) can always be withdrawn tax and penalty-free at any time.
Can I contribute to a Roth IRA if my income is too high? +
Yes, via the backdoor Roth strategy: contribute to a non-deductible Traditional IRA (no income limit), then convert it to a Roth IRA. There are no income limits on conversions. Watch out for the pro-rata rule if you have other pre-tax IRA funds.
When can I withdraw from a Roth IRA without penalty? +
Contributions can be withdrawn any time, tax and penalty-free. Earnings are tax and penalty-free when you are at least 59½ AND the account has been open 5+ years. Withdrawing earnings early incurs income tax plus a 10% penalty (with some exceptions).
Does a Roth IRA have required minimum distributions (RMDs)? +
No. Unlike Traditional IRAs and 401ks, Roth IRAs do not have RMDs during the account owner's lifetime. This makes the Roth IRA an excellent wealth transfer tool — your money can continue to grow tax-free and pass to heirs.
