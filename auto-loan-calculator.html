How to use this auto loan calculator

Enter the vehicle price, your down payment, and any trade-in value. Add your state's sales tax rate, choose a loan term, and enter the APR offered by your lender. Click Calculate to see your monthly payment and total interest.

Tip: Sales tax is usually applied to the vehicle price before subtracting your trade-in value, though this varies by state. The calculator applies tax to the price first.

How monthly car payments are calculated

The loan amount is: (Vehicle Price × (1 + Tax%)) − Down Payment − Trade-in

Then monthly payment uses the standard amortization formula:

M = P · r · (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n − 1)

  • P = net loan amount
  • r = monthly interest rate (APR ÷ 12)
  • n = loan term in months

Example

A $30,000 car with $3,000 down, 7% sales tax, a 60-month term, and 6.5% APR has an amount financed of $29,100, a monthly payment of about $569.37, and total interest of about $5,062.

Tips to lower your car payment

  • Check your credit reports — Correct errors before applying and use actual lender quotes for the APR.
  • Make a larger down payment — This reduces the amount financed and the interest charged.
  • Compare shorter terms — A shorter term raises the payment but usually lowers total interest.
  • Shop multiple lenders — Compare preapprovals with dealer financing using the same amount and term.
  • Negotiate the vehicle price first — Then evaluate financing and optional add-ons separately.

Frequently asked questions

What APR should I enter?

Use the APR from a lender preapproval or financing disclosure. Your actual offer depends on your credit profile, vehicle, term, lender, and market conditions, so a generic rate range is less useful than a quote you can compare.

Should I finance through the dealer or my bank?

Always get a pre-approval from your bank or credit union before visiting the dealer. This gives you a benchmark rate and negotiating leverage. Dealer financing can be competitive, especially with manufacturer incentives, but compare carefully.

What is negative equity on a car loan?

Negative equity (being "underwater") means you owe more on the loan than the car is worth. A larger down payment, a less expensive vehicle, and a shorter term can reduce this risk.

How to compare auto loan offers

The CFPB recommends comparing the full terms of each offer before focusing on the monthly payment. For the same vehicle, collect these numbers from every lender:

  • Out-the-door price, including taxes, registration, dealer fees, and add-ons.
  • Amount financed after the down payment and any trade-in credit.
  • APR and loan term, which together determine the payment and interest.
  • Total of payments and whether a prepayment penalty applies.

For a payment-only comparison across common terms and APR scenarios, use the Car Payment Calculator.

Costs to add outside the loan

This calculator estimates financing, not the full cost of owning the vehicle. Build a separate monthly budget for:

  • Insurance based on a quote for the exact vehicle and drivers.
  • Fuel or charging based on expected mileage and local prices.
  • Maintenance, tires, and repairs.
  • Registration, inspection, parking, and recurring state or local fees.

Do not add these recurring expenses to the loan amount unless the lender actually finances them.
