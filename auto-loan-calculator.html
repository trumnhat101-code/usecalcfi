How to use this auto loan calculator

Enter the vehicle price, your down payment, and any trade-in value. Add your state's sales tax rate, choose a loan term, and enter the APR offered by your lender. Click Calculate to see your monthly payment and total interest.

Tip: Sales tax is usually applied to the vehicle price before subtracting your trade-in value, though this varies by state. The calculator applies tax to the price first.

How monthly car payments are calculated

The loan amount is: (Vehicle Price × (1 + Tax%)) − Down Payment − Trade-in

Then monthly payment uses the standard amortization formula:

M = P · r · (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n − 1)

  • P = net loan amount
  • r = monthly interest rate (APR ÷ 12)
  • n = loan term in months

Example

A $30,000 car with $3,000 down, 7% sales tax, 60-month term at 6.5% APR: loan amount ≈ $29,100, monthly payment ≈ $569, total interest ≈ $5,140.

Tips to lower your car payment

  • Improve your credit score — Even a 50-point increase can drop your rate by 1–2%, saving hundreds over the loan.
  • Make a larger down payment — Putting 20%+ down reduces your loan amount and eliminates negative equity risk.
  • Choose a shorter term — 48 vs. 72 months can cut your total interest cost in half.
  • Shop multiple lenders — Credit unions typically offer rates 1–2% lower than dealership financing.
  • Negotiate the vehicle price first — Dealers may roll extra cost into the interest rate if you negotiate payment first.

Frequently asked questions

What credit score do I need for a good auto loan rate?

Borrowers with scores of 720+ typically qualify for the best rates (under 5% for new vehicles). Scores of 670–719 get near-prime rates. Below 580 is considered subprime, with rates often exceeding 12%.

Should I finance through the dealer or my bank?

Always get a pre-approval from your bank or credit union before visiting the dealer. This gives you a benchmark rate and negotiating leverage. Dealer financing can be competitive, especially with manufacturer incentives, but compare carefully.

What is negative equity on a car loan?

Negative equity (being "underwater") means you owe more on the loan than the car is worth. This happens quickly with long loan terms and small down payments. Avoid it by putting at least 10–20% down and keeping terms at 60 months or less.
