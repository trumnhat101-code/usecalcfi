How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated

The Social Security Administration (SSA) calculates your benefit based on your 35 highest-earning years, adjusted for inflation. This produces your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) — the monthly benefit you receive if you claim exactly at your Full Retirement Age (FRA).

Your actual monthly benefit depends on when you claim relative to your FRA. Claiming early permanently reduces your benefit; claiming late permanently increases it. This calculator uses your estimated FRA benefit and lets you see the impact of any claiming age from 62 to 70.

Early Claiming Reduction Rules

If you claim before your FRA, your benefit is permanently reduced:

  • First 36 months before FRA: reduced by 5/9 of 1% per month (~6.67%/year)
  • Each additional month before FRA: reduced by 5/12 of 1% per month (~5%/year)

For those with FRA of 67 claiming at 62 (60 months early): the reduction is 36 × 5/9% + 24 × 5/12% = 20% + 10% = 30% permanent reduction.

Delayed Retirement Credits (Age 70 Maximum)

For each month you delay past your FRA (up to age 70), your benefit increases by 2/3 of 1% per month — equal to 8% per year. Waiting from FRA 67 to age 70 adds 24% to your monthly benefit permanently. There is no additional credit for waiting beyond age 70.

The Break-Even Analysis: When Does Waiting Pay Off?

Waiting to claim means giving up months of smaller checks to receive larger ones later. The "break-even age" is when your cumulative lifetime benefits become equal regardless of when you claimed.

For most people claiming at FRA vs. 62, the break-even falls around age 80–83. If you expect to live longer than that, waiting usually produces more lifetime income. If you have serious health concerns or need the income now, claiming earlier may be the right choice.

Note: break-even calculations are most useful when comparing two specific ages — not as an absolute rule. Tax implications, spousal benefits, and continued earnings can all shift the optimal strategy.

2026 Social Security Key Facts

  • COLA 2026: Cost-of-living adjustment keeps benefits roughly in line with inflation (check ssa.gov for the latest rate)
  • Maximum benefit at 70 (2026): ~$5,108/month for high earners
  • Average benefit (2026): ~$1,976/month
  • Full retirement age for born 1960+: 67
  • Taxation threshold: Benefits may be taxable if combined income exceeds $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (married)
  • Medicare Part B 2026 premium: Deducted from SS benefit if enrolled; check CMS for current amount

Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security

  • Delay if healthy: Every year past FRA adds 8% permanently — the best guaranteed "investment return" available
  • Coordinate with a spouse: The lower earner often claims early; the higher earner delays to maximize the survivor benefit
  • Bridge with savings: Use retirement accounts or other income to delay SS rather than claiming early out of necessity
  • Check your earnings record: Errors in your SSA record can lower your benefit — review annually at ssa.gov/myaccount
  • Consider taxes: Strategic Roth conversions and income management in your early 60s can reduce how much of your SS is taxable
