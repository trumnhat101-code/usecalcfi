Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 50/30/20 rule? +
The 50/30/20 rule divides your after-tax income into three buckets: 50% for needs (housing, food, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments), 30% for wants (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, travel), and 20% for savings and extra debt payoff. It was popularized by Senator Elizabeth Warren as a simple alternative to detailed line-item budgeting.
Should I use gross or take-home pay? +
Always use after-tax take-home pay — the amount deposited in your bank account. Do not use your gross salary. If your gross is $75,000/year (~$6,250/month), your take-home might be around $4,500–$5,000/month after taxes and pre-tax deductions.
What if my needs exceed 50%? +
Very common in high-cost cities. Adjust the ratio to 60/20/20 or 65/15/20 using the "Needs %" field above — the savings % auto-adjusts. Protect the savings bucket by cutting wants first. Long-term, look to reduce your two biggest needs: housing and transportation.
Does this include 401k contributions? +
If your 401k is pre-tax, those contributions are deducted before your paycheck — they're already out of your take-home and already count toward your savings goal. Roth 401k contributions (post-tax) come out of take-home, so count those in your savings 20%.
Is 20% savings enough for retirement? +
For most people starting by age 30, saving 15–20% of income is sufficient to retire at 65. Starting later? You may need 25–35%. The 20% covers all savings: retirement, emergency fund, and extra debt payoff. See our retirement savings by age guide for benchmarks.
Need vs. want — how do I tell the difference? +
A need is anything where non-payment causes serious consequences: eviction, repossession, utility shutoff, or job loss. Needs: rent, groceries, utilities, car payment (if needed for work), health insurance, minimum debt payments. Wants: dining out, streaming, gym, travel, upgraded phone plan. Test: "If I lost my job tomorrow, would I cancel this immediately?" If yes — it's a want.
