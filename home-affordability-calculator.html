How the home affordability calculator works

Lenders evaluate two DTI ratios when approving a mortgage. This calculator finds the maximum home price that keeps both ratios within guideline limits:

  • Front-end DTI (Housing ratio): Total monthly housing costs ÷ gross monthly income. Most lenders want this ≤ 28%.
  • Back-end DTI (Total DTI): (Housing costs + all other monthly debts) ÷ gross monthly income. Most conventional loans allow up to 36–43%.

The calculator uses the more restrictive of the two limits to determine your maximum affordable home price.

The 28/36 rule explained

The 28/36 rule is a widely used guideline: spend no more than 28% of your gross monthly income on housing and no more than 36% on all debt combined. For example, if you earn $7,500/month:

  • Max housing payment: $7,500 × 28% = $2,100/month
  • Max total debt: $7,500 × 36% = $2,700/month

If you already have $400/month in debt payments, your max housing budget drops to $2,300 (36% − $400), but is still capped by the 28% front-end limit at $2,100.

How to increase your home buying budget

  • Pay down existing debts — Eliminating a $300/month car payment can add $50,000+ to your home budget
  • Increase your down payment — More down = smaller loan = lower monthly payment
  • Improve your credit score — Even 0.5% rate improvement meaningfully increases buying power
  • Add a co-borrower — A spouse or partner's income can significantly expand what you qualify for
  • Choose a 30-year over 15-year loan — Lower monthly payments increase the maximum home price (though total interest is higher)

Frequently asked questions

Does this include PMI?

This calculator does not include PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance). If your down payment is less than 20%, add approximately 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount annually (roughly $50–150/month per $100k borrowed) to your housing cost estimate. PMI can be removed once you reach 20% equity.

What income counts for mortgage qualification?

Lenders count most stable, documented income: salary/wages, self-employment income (2-year average), rental income (usually 75% of gross), Social Security, pension, and consistent bonuses/overtime (2-year history required). Irregular income from gig work or new jobs (under 2 years) may not count or may be averaged.

Should I buy the maximum I can afford?

Probably not. Lender qualification limits are the ceiling, not a recommendation. Budget for maintenance (1–2% of home value annually), unexpected repairs, furniture, and reserves. Many financial advisors recommend spending 20–25% of gross income on housing to maintain financial flexibility.
