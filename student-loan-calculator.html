Federal vs. Private Student Loans: Key Differences

Federal loans offer protections private loans don't: income-driven repayment (IDR), Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), deferment and forbearance options, and fixed interest rates set by Congress each year. Private loans may have lower rates for borrowers with excellent credit, but they lack federal safety nets.

Income-driven repayment plans

Federal IDR plans cap payments at 5–10% of discretionary income and forgive remaining balances after 20–25 years. The SAVE plan (formerly REPAYE) is the most generous for most borrowers. However, forgiven amounts may be taxable as income — a factor to consider when choosing between standard repayment and IDR.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)

If you work for a qualifying government or nonprofit employer, PSLF forgives your remaining federal loan balance after 120 qualifying payments (10 years) on an IDR plan — tax-free. This is the most valuable loan forgiveness program available.

How Extra Payments Accelerate Payoff

Student loan interest accrues daily on your outstanding balance. Every extra dollar you pay reduces principal, which reduces the balance that interest is calculated on each day. On a $30,000 loan at 6.53% over 10 years, your base payment is $338/month. Paying $100 extra ($438 total) saves over $1,800 in interest and cuts 14 months off repayment. Paying $200 extra saves $3,200 and cuts 26 months.

Apply extra payments to your highest-rate loan first if you have multiple loans — this is the debt avalanche method and minimizes total interest paid.

Refinancing Student Loans: When It Makes Sense

Refinancing replaces one or more loans with a new private loan, ideally at a lower rate. It can significantly reduce your monthly payment and total interest. However, refinancing federal loans into a private loan permanently loses all federal protections — IDR plans, PSLF eligibility, and federal deferment options. Only refinance federal loans if you have stable income, don't need federal safety nets, and can get a rate significantly lower than your current rate.

Frequently asked questions

What is the average student loan interest rate?

For 2024–2025, federal undergraduate loans are 6.53%. Graduate unsubsidized loans are 8.08%. PLUS loans are 9.08%. Private loan rates range from 4% to 16%+ depending on credit score and lender.

How long does it take to pay off student loans?

The standard federal plan is 10 years. IDR plans extend to 20–25 years. Making extra payments is the fastest path to payoff under any plan.

Is it worth paying extra on student loans?

Yes — even $50–100/month extra meaningfully reduces total interest and shortens your repayment timeline. If you have high-rate private loans, extra payments are especially valuable since refinancing options may be limited.
