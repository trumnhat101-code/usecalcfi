How self-employment tax works

When you're self-employed, you pay both the employee and employer halves of Social Security and Medicare taxes. That's 15.3% total — but you can deduct half (7.65%) from your taxable income, which saves you money at your marginal income tax rate.

The SE tax calculation steps

  • Net SE income × 92.35% = SE income base (accounts for employer deduction)
  • SE base × 12.4% = Social Security (capped at $176,100 for 2025)
  • SE base × 2.9% = Medicare (no cap; +0.9% surtax over $200k single/$250k MFJ)
  • Deduct ½ of SE tax from gross income before calculating income tax

Key deductions for the self-employed

Beyond the SE tax deduction, self-employed individuals can deduct health insurance premiums, home office, business mileage, retirement contributions (SEP-IRA up to 25% of net earnings), and business expenses. These can significantly lower your tax burden.

Frequently asked questions

When are quarterly estimated taxes due?

Q1: April 15 | Q2: June 16 | Q3: September 15 | Q4: January 15. Missing deadlines can result in an underpayment penalty (typically around 8% annualized on the amount owed).

What if I have both W-2 and self-employment income?

Your W-2 withholding counts toward your estimated tax requirement. Enter your W-2 income above — it affects your income tax bracket but not your SE tax (which applies only to self-employment income).

Top Tax Deductions for Self-Employed Workers

Reducing net self-employment income lowers both your SE tax and income tax. High-value deductions include: Home office — exclusive space used for business qualifies at $5/sq ft (simplified method) or actual expenses. Business mileage — 70 cents per mile driven for business in 2025. Health insurance premiums — fully deductible if no employer plan covers you. SEP-IRA contributions — up to 25% of net earnings, max $69,000. Self-employed 401k — contribute up to $23,500 as employee + 25% of net earnings as employer.

Avoiding Underpayment Penalties

The IRS requires you to pay at least 90% of current-year taxes, or 100% of prior-year taxes (110% if prior AGI exceeded $150k), through withholding and estimated payments. Miss this and you'll owe an underpayment penalty — currently around 8% annualized. The safest approach: use last year's total tax as your target and divide by 4 for quarterly payments. If your income is growing, recalculate each quarter.

SEP-IRA vs. Solo 401k: Retirement Options for the Self-Employed

A SEP-IRA allows contributions up to 25% of net self-employment earnings (max $69,000 for 2025) — easy to set up, no annual filing requirements. A Solo 401k allows the same employer contribution plus a separate employee contribution of up to $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+), making it more powerful for high earners who want to shelter more income. For someone netting $80,000, the Solo 401k could shelter $43,500 vs. $14,650 with a SEP-IRA.
