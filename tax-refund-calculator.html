How the 2025 federal tax refund works

Throughout the year, your employer withholds federal income tax from each paycheck based on the W-4 you filed. At tax time, your actual tax liability is calculated. If you had more withheld than you owe, you get a refund. If less, you owe the difference.

Getting a big refund isn't always good

A large refund means you gave the government an interest-free loan all year. Adjusting your W-4 to withhold less gives you more take-home pay each paycheck — and you can invest or save that money instead. Aim to come close to breaking even at tax time.

Frequently asked questions

How is my tax refund calculated?

Refund = federal tax withheld − actual tax liability. Your liability comes from applying 2025 IRS brackets to your taxable income, then subtracting credits.

Why is my refund smaller than last year?

Common reasons: you updated your W-4, got a raise, had less withholding from a new employer, or your credits changed. A smaller refund often means more accurate withholding throughout the year.

How to Adjust Your Withholding with Form W-4

The W-4 tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold. After major life changes — marriage, divorce, new dependents, a second job — you should file a new W-4. The IRS Tax Withholding Estimator at IRS.gov helps you calculate the right withholding. Enter your expected income, deductions, and credits, and it tells you exactly how many allowances to claim to get close to breaking even at tax time.

Valuable Tax Credits That Reduce What You Owe

Unlike deductions (which reduce taxable income), credits directly reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar. Key 2025 credits: Child Tax Credit — up to $2,000 per child under 17. Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) — up to $7,830 for families with 3+ children. Child and Dependent Care Credit — up to $1,050 (one dependent) or $2,100 (two+). American Opportunity Credit — up to $2,500 for college tuition. These can dramatically reduce your tax liability or increase your refund.

2025 Federal Income Tax Brackets

For single filers in 2025: 10% on income up to $11,925; 12% on $11,926–$48,475; 22% on $48,476–$103,350; 24% on $103,351–$197,300; 32% on $197,301–$250,525; 35% on $250,526–$626,350; 37% above $626,350. For married filing jointly, the income thresholds are approximately double. The US uses a marginal bracket system — only income in each bracket is taxed at that rate, not your entire income.
