How to use this credit card payoff calculator

Enter your current credit card balance, your interest rate (APR), and how much you plan to pay each month. Optionally add an extra monthly amount to see exactly how much interest and time you save. The comparison shows the impact of that extra payment side by side.

The real cost of minimum payments

Credit card minimum payments are designed to keep you paying interest as long as possible. On a $5,000 balance at 22% APR, paying only the minimum (~$100/month) can take over 8 years and cost $4,400+ in interest — nearly doubling what you originally borrowed.

Increasing your payment to $200/month cuts that to about 29 months and roughly $1,700 in interest.

Strategies to pay off credit card debt faster

  • Avalanche method: Pay minimums on all cards, put extra money toward the highest-rate card first. Saves the most interest overall.
  • Snowball method: Pay minimums on all cards, put extra toward the smallest balance first. Builds momentum and motivation.
  • Balance transfer: Move high-rate debt to a 0% intro APR card. Effective if you can pay off the full balance before the promo ends (usually 12–21 months).
  • Personal loan consolidation: Replace 20%+ APR credit card debt with a personal loan at 8–15% APR. Fixed monthly payments make budgeting easier.

Frequently asked questions

What counts as the minimum payment?

Most issuers charge the greater of a flat fee ($25–$35) or 1–2% of your outstanding balance. This calculator estimates your minimum as 2% of the current balance (minimum $25). Your actual minimum varies by issuer — check your statement.

Does paying more than the minimum help my credit score?

Yes — paying down balances reduces your credit utilization ratio (balance ÷ credit limit), which is the second most important factor in your FICO score after payment history. Keeping utilization below 30% (ideally below 10%) improves your score.

Should I pay off credit cards or invest?

If your credit card APR is 15%+, paying it off is almost always the better "investment" since it's a guaranteed return at that rate. Once high-interest debt is cleared, redirect that money to an emergency fund and retirement accounts.

Avalanche vs Snowball: The Numbers

Example: You have $500/month to put toward 3 cards — $3,000 at 22%, $5,000 at 18%, $2,000 at 15%.

MethodAttack OrderTime to Debt-FreeTotal Interest Paid
Avalanche (highest rate first)22% → 18% → 15%~26 months~$1,820
Snowball (lowest balance first)$2K → $3K → $5K~26 months~$2,150

Avalanche saves $330 in this example. Snowball is emotionally motivating (quick wins) but mathematically inferior for high-rate debt.

0% Balance Transfer: Worth It?

If you have good credit (700+), a 0% APR balance transfer card can eliminate interest for 12–21 months. Key math: a 3% transfer fee on $5,000 = $150 upfront. At 22% APR over 18 months, you'd have paid $1,400+ in interest. Net savings: ~$1,250 — a clear win if you pay it off in time.
