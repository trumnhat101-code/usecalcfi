Should you refinance your mortgage?

Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new loan — ideally at a lower interest rate. The key question is whether the long-term savings outweigh the upfront closing costs.

The breakeven rule

Divide your closing costs by your monthly savings to find your breakeven point. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that number of months, refinancing saves money. If you might move sooner, the numbers may not work in your favor.

When refinancing makes sense

  • Your new rate is at least 0.5–1% lower than your current rate
  • You plan to stay past the breakeven point
  • Your credit score has improved since your original loan
  • You want to switch from a 30-year to a 15-year to pay off faster
  • You want to remove PMI by reaching 20% equity

Watch out for

  • Resetting the clock — a new 30-year on a loan you've paid for 10 years adds years of interest
  • Rolling closing costs into the loan increases your balance
  • No-closing-cost loans typically carry a higher rate

Frequently asked questions

When should I refinance my mortgage?

Refinancing typically makes sense when you can lower your rate by at least 0.5–1%, you plan to stay past the breakeven point, and your credit qualifies you for a better rate.

How much does it cost to refinance?

Closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount — usually $3,000–$6,000 on a $200,000 loan. Always factor this into your savings calculation.

What is a breakeven point?

The number of months it takes for monthly savings to offset closing costs. Closing costs $4,000 ÷ savings $200/mo = 20-month breakeven.
