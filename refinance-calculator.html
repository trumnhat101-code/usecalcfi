Should you refinance your mortgage?

Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new loan — ideally at a lower interest rate. The key question is whether the long-term savings outweigh the upfront closing costs.

The breakeven rule

Divide your closing costs by your monthly savings to find your breakeven point. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that number of months, refinancing saves money. If you might move sooner, the numbers may not work in your favor.

When refinancing makes sense

  • Your new rate is at least 0.5–1% lower than your current rate
  • You plan to stay past the breakeven point
  • Your credit score has improved since your original loan
  • You want to switch from a 30-year to a 15-year to pay off faster
  • You want to remove PMI by reaching 20% equity

Watch out for

  • Resetting the clock — a new 30-year on a loan you've paid for 10 years adds years of interest
  • Rolling closing costs into the loan increases your balance
  • No-closing-cost loans typically carry a higher rate

Frequently asked questions

When should I refinance my mortgage?

Refinancing typically makes sense when you can lower your rate by at least 0.5–1%, you plan to stay past the breakeven point, and your credit qualifies you for a better rate.

How much does it cost to refinance?

Closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount — usually $3,000–$6,000 on a $200,000 loan. Always factor this into your savings calculation.

What is a breakeven point?

The number of months it takes for monthly savings to offset closing costs. Closing costs $4,000 ÷ savings $200/mo = 20-month breakeven.

Rate-and-Term vs. Cash-Out Refinance

A rate-and-term refinance replaces your mortgage with a new one at a lower rate or different term — your loan balance stays about the same. A cash-out refinance lets you borrow against your equity, increasing the loan balance to put cash in your pocket. Cash-out refinances typically carry slightly higher rates and should be used carefully since you're converting equity back into debt.

How Your Credit Score Affects Refinance Rates

Even a 20–40 point improvement in your credit score can qualify you for a significantly better mortgage rate. With a 740+ FICO score you'll typically see the best conforming rates. Below 680, many lenders charge risk-based pricing adjustments that can add 0.25–0.75% to your rate. If your score has improved since you took out your original mortgage, refinancing could make strong financial sense.

15-Year vs. 30-Year Refinance

Refinancing from a 30-year to a 15-year mortgage usually gets you a lower rate (often 0.5–0.75% less) and cuts total interest paid dramatically. The tradeoff: monthly payments are substantially higher. On a $300k balance, a 15-year at 6.5% costs ~$2,614/mo vs. a 30-year at 7% at ~$1,996/mo — but you pay ~$170k less in total interest. When Does Refinancing Make Sense?

Refinancing isn't always the right move. Use the breakeven calculation: divide your closing costs by your monthly savings to find how many months until you come out ahead.

✅ Rate drops 0.75%+ and you plan to stay 3+ years

Refinance. Savings outweigh closing costs.

✅ Switch from 30-year to 15-year

Yes, if you can afford the higher payment. Massive interest savings over time.

✅ Remove PMI via refinance (equity 20%+)

Yes. Eliminates ongoing PMI cost immediately.

⚠️ Cash-out refinance

Carefully. Only for high-ROI improvements — you're converting equity back into debt.

❌ Rate drops 0.5% and you plan to sell in 2 years

Probably not. Won't reach breakeven before selling.

Refinance Closing Costs: What to Expect

Refinancing typically costs 2–5% of the loan amount. On a $300,000 loan: $6,000–$15,000. "No-closing-cost" refinances roll fees into the rate — convenient but more expensive long-term.
