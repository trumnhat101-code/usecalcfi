Renting vs buying: the real comparison

The rent-vs-buy decision isn't just about monthly payments. Buying builds equity but comes with large upfront costs (down payment, closing costs), ongoing costs (taxes, insurance, maintenance), and transaction costs when selling. Renting keeps capital liquid for investing.

When buying wins

  • You plan to stay 5+ years (enough time to recoup closing costs)
  • Local home prices are appreciating faster than rent increases
  • Your down payment invested conservatively earns less than home appreciation + equity buildup

When renting wins

  • You may move within 2–3 years (transaction costs eat the gains)
  • Rent-to-price ratio is very favorable in your area
  • You can invest the down payment at higher returns than home appreciation

Frequently asked questions

What is the 5% rule for renting vs buying?

The 5% rule: if annual rent < 5% of home price, renting may be smarter financially. The 5% covers property taxes (~1%), maintenance (~1%), and cost of capital (~3%). Compare: $450k home → $22,500/yr → $1,875/mo threshold.

What costs do people forget when buying?

Closing costs (2–5%), property taxes, HOA fees, maintenance (1–2%/yr), and selling costs (5–6% realtor fees). These significantly impact the true cost of ownership.

The Opportunity Cost of a Down Payment

When you put $80,000 down on a home, that's $80,000 you're not investing in the stock market. If the market returns 8% annually, that $80,000 grows to ~$370,000 over 20 years. Meanwhile, your home might appreciate 3–4% annually. The difference is real money. This doesn't mean renting is always better — home equity is forced savings, and mortgages provide leverage. But the opportunity cost is a key factor renters often ignore when comparing total returns.

How Long You Plan to Stay Is the Most Important Variable

Buying a home and selling within 2–3 years almost always loses money after transaction costs. Real estate agents charge 5–6% of sale price, and closing costs add another 2–3%. On a $400,000 home, you're paying $28,000–$36,000 just to buy and sell — not counting moving costs. The home needs to appreciate significantly just to break even. For stays under 5 years, renting is usually the financially superior choice in most US markets.

Price-to-Rent Ratio by Market

The price-to-rent ratio (home price ÷ annual rent) reveals whether a market favors buying or renting. A ratio below 15 typically favors buying; above 20 favors renting. In 2024, cities like Detroit (ratio ~10) strongly favor buying, while San Francisco (ratio ~35+) and New York (ratio ~25+) strongly favor renting — unless you're committed to staying long-term and believe in local appreciation.
