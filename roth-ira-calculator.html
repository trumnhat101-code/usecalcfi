Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Roth IRA contribution limit for 2026? +
The 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000/year ($583/month) if you're under age 50. If you're 50 or older, you can add a $1,000 catch-up contribution for a total of $8,000/year. This applies to your combined contributions across all IRAs (traditional + Roth combined).
What are the Roth IRA income limits for 2026? +
Single filers: full contribution up to $150,000 MAGI; phased out between $150,000–$165,000; no contribution above $165,000. Married filing jointly: full contribution up to $236,000; phased out $236,000–$246,000; none above $246,000. If you earn too much, consider a backdoor Roth IRA conversion.
Roth IRA vs. traditional IRA — which is better? +
Roth IRA: pay taxes now, all growth and withdrawals are tax-free. Traditional IRA: deduct contributions now, pay taxes on withdrawal. Roth is generally better if you expect your tax rate to be higher in retirement (common for younger, earlier-career workers). Traditional is better if you're in a high bracket now and expect a lower rate in retirement. Both have the same contribution limits.
When can I withdraw from my Roth IRA tax-free? +
You can withdraw your contributions (not earnings) at any time, penalty and tax free. Earnings are tax-free and penalty-free once you're age 59½ AND the account has been open for 5+ years. Early withdrawal of earnings is subject to taxes and a 10% penalty, with exceptions for first home purchase (up to $10,000), disability, and death.
What return rate should I use? +
The S&P 500 has averaged ~10% annually before inflation over the long term. After inflation, that's roughly 7%. Most financial planners recommend 6–7% as a conservative assumption for diversified stock portfolios in long-term projections. This calculator uses nominal (pre-inflation) returns.
Can I contribute to both a Roth IRA and a 401k? +
Yes — contributing to a 401k does not reduce your Roth IRA limit. They have separate limits. For 2026: 401k limit is $23,500 (under 50) or $31,000 (50+). Common strategy: contribute to 401k up to the full employer match first, then max your Roth IRA, then return to 401k with any remaining savings budget.

About the Roth IRA

A Roth IRA is a tax-advantaged retirement account funded with after-tax dollars. Unlike a traditional IRA or 401k, contributions are not tax-deductible — but qualified withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free, including all the investment growth over decades.

Key advantages: Tax-free growth and withdrawals. No required minimum distributions (RMDs) during your lifetime. Contributions can be withdrawn penalty-free any time. Excellent complement to a traditional 401k for tax diversification in retirement.

Best for: Younger workers earlier in their careers, people who expect tax rates to rise, or anyone who wants to hedge against future tax rate increases.
