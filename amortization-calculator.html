How amortization works

Every fixed-rate loan payment is the same amount, but the split between principal and interest shifts over time. Early in the loan, most of each payment is interest. As the balance falls, more goes to principal.

Why the early years are mostly interest

Interest is calculated on the remaining balance. A $320,000 balance at 6.5% generates $1,733 in interest in the first month alone — leaving only $289 of a $2,022 payment to reduce principal. By year 25, that same payment is mostly principal.

Impact of extra payments

Extra principal payments are powerful because they reduce the balance on which future interest is calculated. Even $100/month extra on a 30-year loan can shave 4–5 years off the payoff date and save $30,000+ in interest.

How to Read an Amortization Schedule

Each row shows one payment period with four key columns: Payment (your fixed monthly amount), Principal (amount reducing your balance), Interest (borrowing cost that month), and Balance (remaining loan). In month 1 of a 30-year mortgage, only about 20–25% of your payment goes to principal — the rest is interest. By month 300, over 90% goes to principal.

The crossover point — where principal exceeds interest per payment — typically happens around year 18–20 on a 30-year mortgage. Extra payments made before this point have the greatest impact.

Amortization vs. Interest-Only Loans

A fully amortizing loan (standard mortgage, auto loan, personal loan) pays down principal every month so the balance reaches $0 at term end. An interest-only loan charges just interest for a set period, leaving the full principal balance untouched — then requires a large balloon payment or much higher payments. Most conforming mortgages today are fully amortizing.

Biweekly Payment Strategy

Paying half your monthly payment every two weeks results in 26 half-payments per year — equivalent to 13 full monthly payments instead of 12. That one extra payment per year can shorten a 30-year mortgage by approximately 4 years and save $30,000–$60,000 in interest on a typical loan. Check with your lender to ensure biweekly payments apply to principal directly.
