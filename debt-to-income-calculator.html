What Is Debt-to-Income Ratio and Why It Matters

Your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio is the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes toward debt payments. Lenders use it as one of the primary criteria to evaluate your ability to repay a loan. A lower DTI signals that you have enough income headroom to comfortably handle new debt payments.

Front-end vs. back-end DTI

Front-end DTI (also called the "housing ratio") includes only your proposed housing payment — principal, interest, property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and HOA fees — divided by gross income. Most lenders want this below 28%.

Back-end DTI includes all monthly debt obligations: housing payment plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, personal loans, and any other recurring debt. This is the number lenders focus on most, typically requiring it below 43%.

DTI thresholds by loan type

  • Conventional loans: Max back-end DTI typically 45–50% with excellent credit and reserves
  • FHA loans: Up to 43% standard, up to 57% with compensating factors (large down payment, good credit)
  • VA loans: No hard cap but 41% preferred; residual income requirement also applies
  • USDA loans: Max 41% back-end DTI
  • Jumbo loans: Stricter — typically 43% or lower required

How to Lower Your DTI Before Applying for a Mortgage

If your DTI is too high, you have two levers: reduce debt payments or increase income. The fastest wins come from paying off revolving debt (credit cards) since eliminating a minimum payment immediately drops your DTI. Paying off a $3,000 credit card with a $90/month minimum reduces your back-end DTI by over 1% on a $7,500/month income.

Avoid taking on new debt — car loans, personal loans, or opening new credit cards — in the months before applying. Every new obligation raises your DTI and can delay your mortgage approval.

DTI vs. Credit Score: Which Matters More?

Both matter, but they measure different risks. Your credit score reflects your history of repaying debt (reliability). Your DTI measures current capacity to take on new payments (affordability). A high credit score can sometimes compensate for a slightly elevated DTI — lenders call these "compensating factors." But a DTI over 50% will disqualify you from most conventional loans regardless of your credit score.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good debt-to-income ratio for a mortgage?

Below 36% back-end DTI is considered excellent and will qualify you for the best rates. Up to 43% is acceptable for most loans. Above 43% requires compensating factors or FHA/VA loans.

What is the difference between front-end and back-end DTI?

Front-end DTI is only your housing payment ÷ income. Back-end DTI includes all debt payments ÷ income. Lenders focus primarily on back-end DTI.

How can I lower my DTI ratio?

Pay off credit card balances, eliminate small loans, or add a co-borrower's income. Avoid new debt in the 3–6 months before applying for a mortgage.
